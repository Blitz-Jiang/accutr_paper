\begin{abstract}
    Trust-region-type methods are popular schemes in machine learning due to their excellent practical performance. As accelerating classic algorithms is a fundamental issue in optimization as well as in machine learning, people may wonder if there remains some room for trust-region-type methods to improve. However, the ball-constrained subproblems pose significant obstacles to complexity analysis, making it a daunting task to devise acceleration schemes.
    In this paper, we show for the first time that trust-region-type methods can be accelerated both theoretically and numerically for convex optimization.
    In theory, we prove that our accelerated method has an improved global iteration complexity of $O(\epsilon^{-1/3})$. Numerically, our experiments demonstrate that the proposed algorithm achieves performance comparable to Nesterov's accelerated cubic Newton method, while consistently outperforming non-accelerated trust-region-type methods and the cubic Newton method.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{Intro}
The trust-region (TR) method \cite{More1983,conn2000trust} is recognized as a powerful second-order method due to its effectiveness in solving practical problems and its significant impact across various fields. It has been successfully applied in machine learning applications, such as adversarial neural networks \cite{yao2019trust}, real-time tracking \cite{liu2004real}, and mixed norm regression \cite{kim2010scalable}. Notably, the trust-region method has also inspired the widely used trust-region policy optimization \cite{schulman2015trust, kurutach2018model} in the reinforcement learning community.

In this paper, we consider the unconstrained optimization problem \begin{equation} \label{eq.main problem} f^* = \min_{x\in \mathbb{R}^n}~ f(x), \end{equation} where $f:\mathbb{R}^n\to \mathbb{R}$ is convex with its Hessian being Lipschitz continuous, and there exists a minimizer $x^* \in \mathbb{R}^n$ such that $f(x^*)=f^*$. 
Surprisingly, despite the long history and its success in solving various practical problems, the performance of the trust-region method when applied to the above convex problem is largely unknown, and the current research mostly focuses on nonconvex optimization. 

Moreover, even for nonconvex optimization, its theoretical underpinnings are still evolving, and complexity analyses have proven challenging.
Historically, the convergence analyses of the standard trust-region method were only for nonconvex optimization and the established convergence rate was primarily asymptotic ~\cite{conn2000trust, yuan2015recent}. In terms of nonasymptotic convergence, Early studies established that the standard trust-region method \cite{nocedal1999numerical,conn2000trust,cartisComplexitySteepestDescent2010,cartisComplexityBoundsSecondorder2012} converges to stationary points with an iteration complexity of $O(\epsilon^{-2})$, being suboptimal in terms of second-order methods \cite{carmon2021lower}. Recently, to achieve the optimal iteration complexity of $O(\epsilon^{-3/2})$, various new variants of trust-region methods with different algorithmic design and analytical techniques~\cite{luenberger_linear_2021, curtis2017trust, curtis2021trust, hamad2022consistently, hamad2024simplepracticaladaptivetrustregion} have been proposed.

However, there are many machine learning problems that are indeed convex, such as LASSO regression~\cite{ranstam2018lasso}, logistic regression~\citet{lavalley2008logistic}, and the principle component analysis~\cite{abdi2010principal}. In addition, algorithms and corresponding analyses for convex optimization are also effective tools for nonconvex problems~\cite{carmon2017convex,carmon2018accelerated,li2023restarted}. A very recent work~\cite{jiang2024nonconvexityuniversaltrustregionmethod} shows that the trust-region-type method can perform as well as other effective second-order methods both theoretically and numerically for convex problems. People may wonder if there remains some room for trust-region-type methods to improve.
Note that one fundamental issue in optimization as well as in machine learning is understanding how the classical algorithms can be accelerated. In the context of convex optimization, acceleration techniques~\cite{nesterovMethodSolvingConvex1983a,nesterov_lectures_2018} have emerged as a critical aspect of the research, due to theoretical and practical successes of accelerated first-order methods in machine learning~\cite{lan_first-order_2020,lin2020accelerated}. Recently, The wave of research on accelerated algorithms has already reached the domain of second-order methods \cite{nesterov2008accelerating, monteiro2013accelerated,doikov2020contracting,carmon2022optimal,NEURIPS2022_e56f394b}.
Therefore, based on the results in \citet{jiang2024nonconvexityuniversaltrustregionmethod}, a natural question arises for advancing the theoretical understanding of trust-region-type methods:
    \begin{center}
        \emph{Can trust-region-type methods be accelerated for convex optimization?}
    \end{center}
In terms of accelerated second-order methods, \citet{nesterov2008accelerating} showed that it is possible to accelerate the cubic regularized Newton method~\cite{nesterov2006cubic}. Specifically, its iteration complexity can be improved to $O(\epsilon^{-1/3})$ from $O(\epsilon^{-1/2})$ for convex problems. Beyond cubic regularization, other accelerated second-order approaches have been developed~\cite{monteiro2013accelerated,doikov2020contracting,carmon2022optimal,NEURIPS2022_e56f394b}. Those algorithms have also spawned a series of studies, each focusing on different aspects, to name a few, see~\citet{jiang2020unified,jiang2021optimal,chen2022accelerating,lin2022explicit,agafonov2023inexact,agafonov2024advancing,jiang2024accelerated,huang2024inexact}. 
\paragraph{Challenge for acceleration with constraint}
It is worth mentioning that all the above accelerated methods stick with \emph{unconstrained subproblems}.
However, trust-region-type methods remain obviously absent from this landscape in the presence of the ball constraint of trust-region subproblems, which makes the analysis more challenging. To illustrate the difficulty of accelerating trust-region-type methods, we recall a key property required for acceleration, referred to as the \emph{sufficient decrease condition} (Definition~\ref{def.sufficient decrease condition}) in this paper. This condition plays a crucial role in enabling the estimate sequence technique \cite{baesEstimateSequenceMethods2009a,nesterov_lectures_2018}, which recursively connects the function values among the previous iterations, ensuring consistent progress that ultimately leads to acceleration. The sufficient decrease condition is fundamentally tailored for the accelerated scheme.

This technique relies on establishing a recursive relationship involving the function value across iterations, ensuring consistent cumulative progress that ultimately leads to acceleration. The cumulative progress in function value is naturally ensured by the sufficient decrease condition, which is specifically designed to support this kind of iterative setting.

As we shall see later, the accelerated algorithms, such as the accelerated cubic regularized Newton method \cite{nesterov2008accelerating}, benefit from the key that the regularizer of the update is naturally proportional to the step size \eqref{eq.cropt}. This property clears the barrier as such the sufficient decrease condition would hold automatically, and thus justifies the implementation of estimate sequence technique.

In contrast, the ball constraint in the trust-region-type methods introduces posterior multipliers, creating complex dependencies between the step size and the regularizer\cite{nocedal1999numerical,cartis2022evaluation}. Consequently, trust-region-type methods do not consistently satisfy the sufficient decrease condition, failing to guarantee the basic requirements for acceleration.
\paragraph{Our approach}
To address the challenge of the incompatibility between trust-region-type methods and the sufficient decrease condition, we adopt and modify the trust-region subproblem introduced by~\citet{jiang2024nonconvexityuniversaltrustregionmethod}. This kind of subproblem has the nice property of two-mode progress: it can guarantee either the function value decreases sufficiently or the gradient norm shrinks linearly after one update. Based on this property, we design a customized acceleration method (Algorithm~\ref{alg.accelerated utr}) that effectively integrates these two complementary modes of progress: instead of forcing every iteration to satisfy the sufficient decrease condition, we adopt a different roadmap to make the estimate sequence technique applicable. Specifically, when the function value decreases sufficiently, we bypass the verification of the sufficient decrease condition and directly proceed with the update. In cases where the gradient norm contracts, we incorporate the local detection subroutine (Algorithm~\ref{alg.rr}) into Algorithm~\ref{alg.accelerated utr}, ensuring that either an approximate solution or a step satisfying the sufficient decrease condition is obtained.

\paragraph{Contribution}
In this paper, we proposed the first accelerated method (\cref{alg.accelerated utr}) that can accommodate trust-region-type subproblems. We prove the proposed method achieves the improved global iteration complexity of $O(\epsilon^{-1/3})$, which matches that of the accelerated cubic regularized Newton method~\cite{nesterov2008accelerating}, for finding $\epsilon$-approximate solutions in convex optimization. The number of subproblems required in each iteration scales logarithmically with the desired accuracy, resulting in a total complexity of $\tilde{O}(\epsilon^{-1/3})$.

In terms of the algorithm design, our method sets the trust-region radius proportional to the square root of the gradient norm at certain points and avoids updating the radius by the accept-or-reject mechanism in the standard trust-region method. The design and incorporation of the local detection subroutine (Algorithm~\ref{alg.rr}) is also new in accelerated algorithms and may have the potential for independent study. It helps the algorithm stop early, or switch from one mode of progress (gradient norm decrease) to the other mode of progress (function value decrease) and finally achieve acceleration.



% we apply the estimate sequence technique for the first time to accelerate trust-region type algorithms, which involve a constrained subproblem that needs special handling. In particular, we design the first accelerated trust-region-type method that achieves a global iteration complexity of $O(\epsilon^{-1/3})$ for finding $\epsilon$-approximate solutions in convex optimization. We remark that the number of subproblems we solve in each iteration scales logarithmically with the desired accuracy, resulting in a total complexity of $\tilde{O}(\epsilon^{-1/3})$. Notably, the subproblems within the same iteration share a similar structure, minimizing the additional computational burden.

Numerical experiments demonstrate the efficacy of our method. It is comparable to the accelerated cubic Newton method~\cite{nesterov2008accelerating} and outperforms the non-accelerated counterparts of both the trust-region method for convex optimization~\cite{jiang2024nonconvexityuniversaltrustregionmethod} and the accelerated cubic Newton method~\cite{nesterov2006cubic}.
\paragraph{Related Works}
We now provide a literature review, with a focus on further discussing the accelerated second-order algorithms mentioned earlier. Apart from the accelerated cubic Newton method, another significant development in the accelerated second-order method is the MS acceleration framework proposed by~\cite{monteiro2013accelerated} and further developed by~\citet{NEURIPS2022_e56f394b,carmon2022optimal}, which serves as a general approach for incorporating second-order methods as inner oracles, achieving an iteration complexity of $O(\epsilon^{-2/7})$. \citet{carmon2022optimal} further demonstrated that some second-order methods qualify as oracles within the MS framework.

More recently, a new second-order method employing gradient regularization was introduced by~\citet{mishchenko_regularized_2023}. This approach can be accelerated using the contracting mapping technique~\cite{doikov2020contracting}, leading to the development of the accelerated gradient regularized Newton method, which achieves a global iteration complexity of $O(\epsilon^{-1/3})$. In each iteration, they also solve a number of subproblems whose quantity scales logarithmically with the desired accuracy, but unlike ours, these subproblems lack a common structure. To the best of our knowledge, the work \cite{carmon2020acceleration} is the only one that addresses accelerating algorithms with a constrained subproblem. However, their iteration bound is based on a quite different metric, and their subproblem asks for minimization of the original objective function within a trust-region ball, which makes it still challenging to solve. These lead to significant differences in how the complexity is measured and analyzed.

Let $\|\cdot\|$ denote the standard Euclidean norm in $\mathbb{R}^n$, and let $x^T y$ and $\langle x,y\rangle$ represent the inner product between $x, y \in \mathbb{R}^n$. For a matrix $X \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$, $\|X\|$ denotes the induced $\ell_2$ norm. We use the standard asymptotic notations $O(\cdot), \Omega(\cdot), \Theta(\cdot)$ in their conventional sense, where $\tilde{O}(\cdot)$ hides logarithmic terms within $O(\cdot)$. Specifically, for two constants $A$ and $B$, we say that $A = O(B)$ if there exists a constant $c > 0$ such that $A \leq c \cdot B$, and $A = \Omega(B)$ if there exists a constant $c > 0$ such that $A \geq c \cdot B$. We write $A = \Theta(B)$ if both $A = O(B)$ and $A = \Omega(B)$ hold.

\begin{assumption}
    \label{assm.main assm}
    The objective function $f: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ is twice continuously differentiable, and its Hessian is Lipschitz continuous. That is, there exists a constant $M > 0$ such that for all $x, y \in \mathbb{R}^n$,
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq.main assm}
        \|\nabla^2 f(x) - \nabla^2 f(y)\| \leq M \|x - y\|. \end{equation}
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}
    \label{assm.bounded hessian}
    The Hessian of the objective function is bounded. Specifically, there exists a constant $\kappa_H > 0$ such that for all $x \in \mathbb{R}^n$, \begin{equation}
        \label{eq.bounded hessian}
        \|\nabla^2 f(x)\| \leq \kappa_H.
    \end{equation} \end{assumption}


We are interested in finding $\epsilon$-approximate solutions as below.

\begin{definition}
    \label{def.approximate solution}
    Given $\epsilon > 0$, a point $x \in \mathbb{R}^n$ is an $\epsilon$-approximate solution to problem~\eqref{eq.main problem} if
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq.approximate solution}
        f(x) - f^* \leq \epsilon \quad \text{or} \quad \|\nabla f(x)\| \leq \epsilon.
    \end{equation}
\end{definition}
\paragraph{Subproblems in second-order methods}
We employ trust-region-type methods to find the approximate solution of~\eqref{eq.main problem} as in Definition~\ref{def.approximate solution}.
the standard trust-region method \cite{conn2000trust} operates on the local quadratic approximation of $f$ at point $x\in\mathbb{R}^n$ within a trust-region ball:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq.classic tr subproblem} \begin{aligned} \min_{d \in \mathbb{R}^n} \   m^{\text{TR}}(d)&:= \nabla f(x)^T d+\tfrac{1}{2}d^T \nabla^2 f(x) d, \\ \operatorname{s.t.} \ &\|d\|\le \Delta.
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}
The solution to this subproblem is distinguished by the step $d$ and the multiplier $\lambda$ to the ball constraint.
To accelerate the trust-region-type method, in this paper we modify the subproblem from~\citet{jiang2024nonconvexityuniversaltrustregionmethod}, which has the following form:
\begin{subequations}\label{eq.newTR}
    \begin{align}
        \min_{d \in \mathbb{R}^n} m^{\text{SA}}(d) &:=\nabla f(x)^T d + \tfrac{1}{2}d^T (\nabla^2 f(x) + \sigma I) d, \\\label{eq.sa.ballconstraint} \operatorname{s.t.} ~& \|d\| \le r.
    \end{align}
\end{subequations}
% Similar to their paper, this formulation combines both an $\ell_2$-regularizer and the trust-region constraint. However, the key modification lies in the increased flexibility of the trust-region subproblem, allowing greater freedom in adjusting the iteration-dependent parameters $\sigma$ and $r$. Specifically, we no longer require these parameters to be linked to the norm of the current gradient. We will demonstrate that these parameters can be chosen in a way that is more suitable for acceleration.

The superscript \textrm{SA} is mnemonic for Subproblem for Acceleration.
In~\citet{jiang2024nonconvexityuniversaltrustregionmethod}, the parameter $(\sigma, r)$ is set to some multiples of $\|\nabla f(x)\|^{1/2}$, which turns out to be inappropriate for acceleration. In this paper we no longer tight them to $\nabla f(x)$, allowing even greater freedom to work with the estimate sequence technique.


We now write the optimality conditions of \eqref{eq.newTR}. The proof is omitted for brevity since it is analogous to the one for \eqref{eq.classic tr subproblem} outlined in~\citet[Section 3]{conn2000trust}.
\begin{lemma}\label{lem.optimal condition}
    The direction $d$ is the solution to \eqref{eq.newTR} if and only if there exists a dual multiplier $\lambda \geq 0$ of the constraint \eqref{eq.sa.ballconstraint} such that: \begin{subequations}\label{eq.sa}
        \begin{align}
            \label{eq.optcond primal}
             & \| d\| \leq r                                                       \\
            \label{eq.optcond coml slack}
             & \lambda \left(\|d\|-r \right)=0                                     \\ \label{eq.optcond firstorder}
             & \left(\nabla^2 f(x) + \sigma I + \lambda I \right) d = -\nabla f(x) \\
            \label{eq.optcond secondorder}
             & \nabla^2 f(x) + \sigma I + \lambda I \succeq 0.
        \end{align}
    \end{subequations}
\end{lemma}

For convenience, we denote the primal-dual solution pair of \eqref{eq.newTR} by $(d,\lambda) = \operatorname{SA}(x, \sigma, r)$.

Apparently, among all the above conditions, \eqref{eq.optcond firstorder} forms the decisive computations of step $d$. In fact, most second-order methods update the step at $x$ in a similar form as follows:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq.second-order update}
    \left (\nabla^2 f(x) + \mu I \right )d = -\nabla f(x),
\end{equation}
where $\mu$ is a generic regularizer that varies among different methods. Apparently, if $\mu=\lambda$ we recover the step of \eqref{eq.classic tr subproblem}, if $\mu = \sigma+\lambda$ we recover the step of \eqref{eq.newTR}.

In contrast, another milestone second-order method, the cubic regularized Newton method (CRM)~\cite{nesterov2006cubic}, has the following subproblem:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq.cr update}
    \min_{d\in\mathbb{R}^n} m^{\text{CR}}(d):=\nabla f(x)^Td+\tfrac{1}{2}d^T\nabla^2 f(x)d+\tfrac{M}{6}\|d\|^3,
\end{equation}
The optimality condition of \eqref{eq.cr update} reads
\begin{equation}\label{eq.cropt}
    (\nabla^2 f(x) + \tfrac{M}{2}\|d\| I)d = -\nabla f(x).
\end{equation}
As we can see, in the CRM update we have $\mu = \frac{M}{2}\|d\|$ in view of \eqref{eq.cropt}.

% Next, we discuss the relationship between the \eqref{eq.second-order update} and acceleration in earlier literature. When it comes to acceleration, an unavoidable proposition is the sufficient decrease condition, which is mentioned earlier and is the desirable condition for second-order methods to be accelerated.
\paragraph{Acceleration, estimate sequence, and sufficient decrease}

The rest of this section is to discuss the requirements imposed on \eqref{eq.second-order update} so as to devise an accelerated method.
Firstly, we briefly discuss the estimate sequence technique. The essential idea behind this strategy is to maintain a sequence $\{\phi_k(x),A_k,v_k,x_k\}$ to approximate the objective function $f(x)$ from both above and below. In the algorithm, we only update $x_k$ and $v_k$, while the auxiliary sequences $\phi_k(x)$ and $A_k$ are used in the analyses without explicit computations. Specifically, the following relations must be satisfied for each $k$:
\begin{subequations}\label{eq.es relation}
\begin{align}
    \label{eq.es relation 1}
     & \mathcal{R}_k^1: \ \phi_k^*  \geq A_k f(x_k), \  \phi_k^*= \phi_k(v_k) =\min_{x\in\mathbb{R}^n}\phi_k(x) \\
    \label{eq.es relation 2}
     & \mathcal{R}_k^2: \ \phi_k(x)\leq A_kf(x)+\phi_0(x), \ \forall x\in\mathbb{R}^n.
\end{align}
\end{subequations}
By setting $x = x^*$ in \eqref{eq.es relation 2}, it follows from \eqref{eq.es relation 1} and \eqref{eq.es relation 2} that
\begin{equation*}
    f(x_k)-f^* \leq \tfrac{1}{A_k}\phi_0(x^*).
\end{equation*}
Thus, the complexity rate follows how rapidly $A_k$ grows. To accelerate the CRM, \citet{nesterov2008accelerating} proposed an efficient approach where $A_k=\Theta(k^3)$, leading to an iteration complexity of $O(\epsilon^{-1/3})$. The fact is, in almost all accelerated schemes, to ensure $\mathcal{R}_k^1$ \eqref{eq.es relation 1}, the subproblems should be solved to fulfill the following condition.

\begin{definition}[Sufficient decrease condition]
    \label{def.sufficient decrease condition}
    For function $f$, at $x\in\mathbb{R}^n$, we say that a step $d$ satisfies the sufficient decrease condition if
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq.inner product g d advanced}
        \langle \nabla f(x+d),-d \rangle \geq \Omega\left(\|\nabla f(x+d)\|^{3/2}\right).
    \end{equation}
\end{definition}
For convex function $f(x)$, \eqref{eq.inner product g d advanced} implies the following:
\begin{equation*}
    \begin{aligned}
        f(x)-f(x+d) & \geq \langle \nabla f(x+d),-d \rangle            \\
                    & \geq \Omega\left(\|\nabla f(x+d)\|^{3/2}\right).
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation*}
Although we use this condition in a more complex manner in the proof, the above inequality justifies the name of the condition. To make \eqref{eq.inner product g d advanced} hold, a sufficient condition was proposed by~\citet[Lemma 6]{nesterov2008accelerating}, which only applies naturally to CRM. The modification below makes it compatible with any other second-order methods that take the update via \eqref{eq.second-order update}. The sufficient condition is based on the following \emph{ratio pass} regarding the regularizer $\mu$ and the step size $\|d\|$.
\begin{lemma}[Ratio pass]
    \label{lem.ratiopass}
    Suppose Assumption~\ref{assm.main assm} holds, and \eqref{eq.second-order update} holds for some $\tfrac{\mu}{\|d\|} = \Theta(1)$, then $d$ satisfies the sufficient decrease condition at $x$.
\end{lemma}

\paragraph{Why the roadmap fails for trust-region-type methods}
In the case of trust-region-type algorithms, the regularizer $\mu$ is a function of the multiplier coming from the ball constraint \eqref{eq.sa.ballconstraint}.  
In the standard trust-region subproblem \eqref{eq.classic tr subproblem}, the ratio is computed as $\tfrac{\lambda}{\|d\|}$. For our subproblem~\eqref{eq.newTR}, we have instead $\tfrac{\sigma + \lambda}{\|d\|}$. 
Such ratio $\frac{\mu(\lambda)}{\|d\|}$ is not at all stable in the iterates.

\section{The Accelerated Trust-Region Method}\label{sec.method}
In this section, we introduce the Accelerated Trust-Region (\texttt{ATR}) method (Algorithm~\ref{alg.accelerated utr}), along with its auxiliary local detection subroutine (Algorithm~\ref{alg.rr}), and provide an explanation of the intuition behind their design.

% As discussed in Section~\ref{sec.priliminary}, we must capture the essential characteristics of the trust-region-type subproblem to design an algorithm that can accelerate it even when the sufficient decrease condition does not hold.

As discussed in the previous section, the sufficient decrease condition cannot be inherently guaranteed for our subproblem \eqref{eq.newTR}. Fortunately, the subproblem possesses exploitable characteristics that can serve as an alternative. Specifically, with careful choice of $(\sigma_k,r_k)$, the progress made therein can be interpreted by two distinct cases. We give the demonstration in the following two lemmas: when $\lambda_k>0$, we readily achieve a sufficient decrease in the function value; when $\lambda_k = 0$, we establish a bound on the gradient norm at the next iteration.

\begin{lemma}
    \label{lem.function decrease}
    Suppose Assumption~\ref{assm.main assm} holds and $(d,\lambda) = \operatorname{SA}(x,\sigma,r)$. When $\sigma \geq M r$ and $\lambda>0$, we have
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq.function decrease advanced}
        f(x+d) \leq f(x)-\frac{1}{2}d^T \nabla^2 f(x) d-\lambda r^2 -\frac{5M}{6} r^3.
    \end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{lemma}
    \label{lem.bound next gradient}
    Suppose Assumption~\ref{assm.main assm} holds and $(d,\lambda)= \operatorname{SA}(x,\sigma,r)$ , when $\lambda=0$, we have
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq.bound next gradient}
        \|\nabla f(x+d)\| \leq \frac{M}{2}r^2+\sigma r.
    \end{equation}
\end{lemma}



\begin{algorithm}[ht]
    \caption{Accelerated Trust-Region Method (\atr{})}\label{alg.accelerated utr}
    \begin{algorithmic}[1]
        \STATE \textbf{input:} $x_0=v_0\in \mathbb{R}^n$, $s_0=0$
        \FOR{$k=0, 1, \ldots, K_\epsilon$}
        \STATE $y_k = \frac{k}{k+3}x_k+\frac{3}{k+3}v_k$
        \STATE $(\sigma_k,r_k) = (\tfrac{\sqrt{M}}{3}\|\nabla f(y_k)\|^{1/2},\tfrac{1}{3\sqrt{M}}\|\nabla f(y_k)\|^{1/2})$
        \STATE $(d_k,\lambda_k) = \operatorname{SA}(y_k,\sigma_k,r_k)$ \hfill\texttt{\# See \eqref{eq.sa}}
        % \noindent\rule{8cm}{0.4pt} 
        \IF{$\lambda_k>0$} \label{line.if}
        % \STATE $x_{k+1}=y_k+d_k$ \label{line.nonzero multiplier}
        \STATE \textbf{\# Case 1: Function Value Decrease}
        \STATE $x_{k+1}=y_k+d_k$
        \STATE $s_{k+1} = s_{k}+\frac{(k+1)(k+2)}{2}\nabla f(y_k)$
        \ELSE \label{line.else}
        \STATE \textbf{\# Case 2: Local Detection}
        \STATE ($d_k,\textrm{ET}) = \operatorname{LD}(y_k,d_k)$ \hfill\texttt{\# Algorithm 2}
        \STATE \textbf{if}~\textrm{ET}\textbf{; then terminate; end if}
        % \IF{$\textrm{ET}$} \STATE\textbf{terminate} \ENDIF
        \STATE $x_{k+1}=y_k+d_k$
        \STATE $s_{k+1} = s_{k}+\tfrac{(k+1)(k+2)}{2}\nabla f(x_{k+1})$
        \ENDIF
        \STATE $v_{k+1} = v_0-\sqrt{\tfrac{\ell}{3M\|s_{k+1}\|}}s_{k+1}$
        \ENDFOR
    \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[ht]  \caption{Local Detection for ATR (LD)}\label{alg.rr}
    \begin{algorithmic}[1]
        \STATE \textbf{Input: } $y_k,d_k^{(-1)} := d_k\in\mathbb{R}^n$
        \FOR{$j= 0,1,\ldots,j_k$}
        \STATE \textbf{\# Phase 1: Linear Contraction}
        \STATE $\xi^{(j)} = \|\nabla f(y_k+d_k^{(j-1)})\|^{1/2}$
        \IF{$\xi^{(j)}\leq \sqrt \epsilon$}
        \STATE \textbf{output} $d_k^{(j-1)}$, $\textrm{ET}=\text{True}$ 
        \ENDIF
        \STATE $(\sigma_k^{(j)},r_k^{(j)}) = (\frac{\sqrt{M}}{3}\xi^{(j)},\frac{1}{3\sqrt{M}}\xi^{(j)})$
        \STATE $(d_k^{(j)},\lambda_k^{(j)})=\operatorname{SA}(y_k,\sigma_k^{(j)},r_k^{(j)})$
        \STATE \textbf{\# Phase 2: Regularity Refinement}
        \IF{$0<\lambda_k^{(j)}\leq \frac{\sqrt{M}}{3}\xi^{(j)}$} \label{line.bisection}
        \STATE \textbf{output } $d_k^{(j)}, \textrm{ET}=\text{False}$ \label{line.zero multiplier 1}
        \ELSIF{$\lambda_k^{(j)}> \frac{\sqrt{M}}{3}\xi^{(j)}$}
        \STATE $\mu_- = \tfrac{\sqrt{M}}{3}\xi^{(j)}, \mu_+ = \lambda_k^{(j)}+\tfrac{\sqrt{M}}{3}\xi^{(j)}$ \label{line.bracketing}
        \STATE $d_k^{(j)} = \operatorname{BS}(y_k,\mu_-,\mu_+)$ \label{line.zero multiplier 2} \texttt{\# Algorithm 3}
        \STATE \textbf{output } $d_k^{(j)}, \textrm{ET}=\text{False}$
        \ENDIF \label{line.endbisection}
        \ENDFOR
    \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

We present Algorithm~\ref{alg.accelerated utr}, which governs the recursive relations $\mathcal{R}_k^1,\mathcal{R}_k^2$; cf.~\eqref{eq.es relation}. Following the guidance of Lemma~\ref{lem.function decrease} and Lemma~\ref{lem.bound next gradient}, a natural approach is to separate the iterations into \textbf{Case 1} and \textbf{Case 2} shown in Algorithm~\ref{alg.accelerated utr}, where different strategies are adopted for updating the estimate sequence. 
% The former case to ensure that either the recursive relation smoothly transitions to the next iteration, or the algorithm terminates early (the flag $\textrm{ET}$ stands for early terminate). This distinction is the key difference between our algorithm and other acceleration frameworks.

Specifically, in \textbf{Case 1} of Algorithm~\ref{alg.accelerated utr}, the multiplier $\lambda_k>0$ indicates that the constraint is active and our choice of $(\sigma_k,r_k)$ already brought a sufficient decrease in the order of  $\Theta(\|\nabla f(y_k)\|^{3/2})$. 
\begin{corollary}\label{coro.function value decrease}
    Suppose Assumption~\ref{assm.main assm} holds, in \textbf{Case~1} of Algorithm~\ref{alg.accelerated utr}, we have
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq.decrease of order 1.5}
        f(x_{k+1}) \leq f(y_k)-\frac{5}{162\sqrt{M}}\|\nabla f(y_k)\|^{3/2}.
    \end{equation}
\end{corollary}
By virtue of the above corollary, we are free to directly update the estimate sequence and the recursive relations pass down the next iterate; there is no need for verification of ratio pass and sufficient decrease condition.

On the opposite, for \textbf{Case 2} we design a Local Detection (LD) process (\Cref{alg.rr}) that produces a refined direction $d_k$ based on local properties in the iterate $y_k$, which follows two sequential phases.

In \textbf{Phase 1} of Algorithm~\ref{alg.rr}, an inner loop indexed by $j$ is used, where we keep trying $\xi^{(j)}_k$ as long as $\lambda_k^{(j)}=0$. 
By \cref{lem.bound next gradient} it implies linear contraction of $\|\nabla f(y_k+d_k^j)\|$. 
\begin{corollary}
    \label{coro.inner loop bound next gradient}
    Suppose Assumption~\ref{assm.main assm} holds, if in the $j$-th iteration, Algorithm~\ref{alg.rr} does not output $d_k^{(j)}$ or $\textrm{ET}$, then
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq.inner loop bound next gradient}
        (\xi^{(j+1)})^2 \leq \tfrac{1}{6}(\xi^{(j)})^2.
    \end{equation}
\end{corollary}
Intuitively, we hope the contraction continues and thus speeds up the convergence. Ideally, the gradient norm converges linearly until a sign ``ET'' (Early Termination) is produced and we will arrive at an $\epsilon$-approximate solution. 

Otherwise, the last $\xi^{(j)}$ causing $\lambda_k^{(j)} \neq 0$ is collected. We turn to \textbf{Phase 2} for a step $d_k^{(j)}$ such that the sufficient decrease condition (\cref{def.sufficient decrease condition}) holds. Specifically, after the nonzero $\lambda_k^{(j)}$ occurs, if it is sufficiently small, LD directly outputs $d_k^{(j)}$. If not, the interval $[\mu_-,\mu_+]$ below provides a good estimate where the ratio pass (\cref{lem.ratiopass}) is possibly true.
\begin{equation*}
    % \begin{aligned}
         % & \|d_k^{(j)}\| = \tfrac{1}{3\sqrt{M}}\xi^{(j)}, \\
         \mu_- = \tfrac
        {\sqrt{M}}{3}\xi^{(j)}, \ \mu_+= \lambda_k^{(j)} + \tfrac{\sqrt{M}}{3}\xi^{(j)}.
    % \end{aligned}
\end{equation*}
The rest for us to do is to utilize these ``good predictions'' to strictly locate some $\mu_k$, such that the ratio $\tfrac{\mu_k}{\|d_k\|}\in[M, 2M]$ and hence the sufficient decrease condition follows. Luckily, a standard bisection search procedure (Algorithm~\ref{alg.bisection}) can serve this purpose.

\begin{algorithm}[!ht]
    \caption{Bisection for ATR (BS)}\label{alg.bisection}
    \begin{algorithmic}[1]
        \STATE{\textbf{input:} $y\in \mathbb{R}^n$, $\mu_- < \mu_+$;}
        \WHILE{True}
        \STATE{$\mu = \tfrac{\mu_-+\mu_+}{2}$}
        \STATE{$d = -\left (\nabla ^2 f(y)+\mu I\right )^{-1}\nabla f(y)$}
        \IF{$\frac{\mu}{\|d\|}<M$}
        \STATE{$\mu_-=\mu$}
        \ELSIF{$\tfrac{\mu}{\|d\|}>2M$}
        \STATE{$\mu_+ = \mu$}
        \ELSE
        \STATE{\textbf{output} $d$}
        \ENDIF
        \ENDWHILE
    \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

% Our accelerated Algorithm~\ref{alg.accelerated utr} is capable of automatically adjusting the iteration process based on the local properties of the current point, offering tailored methods for different local characteristics. Remarkably, we use the sufficient decrease condition as a final safeguard instead of forcing every step to satisfy it.
In summary, \cref{alg.accelerated utr} is designed by exploiting the dual multipliers of constrained subproblems. One can see that the traditional Roadmap~\ref{fig.standard roadmap} is only the last insurance for our acceleration scheme; the linearly contracting \textbf{Phase 1} is novel and when the function value decrease is sufficient, we can bypass the traditional roadmap and guarantee the recursive relation in estimate sequence directly.

\section{Convergence Analysis}\label{sec.convergence}
In this section, we analyze the complexity of \cref{alg.accelerated utr}. Let $K_\epsilon$ denote the index of the first iterate that satisfies Definition~\ref{def.approximate solution}. We will show that the number of iterations needed is $K_\epsilon = O(\epsilon^{-1/3})$, and the number of trust-region-subproblems solved within these iterations is bounded by $\tilde{O}(\epsilon^{-1/3})$. We note that, in the worst-case case, an additional logarithmic number of trust-region subproblems may have to be solved. However, since the Hessian and gradient of these subproblems remain the same, the additional computational cost is minimal.


First, we formally divide the iterates produced by Algorithm~\ref{alg.accelerated utr} into two sets:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq.outer index catagory}
    \begin{aligned}
         & \mathcal{N} = \left \{ k | k\leq K_\epsilon,\ x_k \text{\ updated in \textbf{Case 1}}\right\}, \\
         & \mathcal{Z} = \left \{k |k\leq K_\epsilon,\ x_k \text{\ updated in \textbf{Case 2}}\right\}.
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}
As seen in \cref{coro.function value decrease}, \textbf{Case 1} is a good case since it directly guarantees the validity of the estimate sequence.

In \textbf{Case 2}, Algorithm~\ref{alg.accelerated utr} invokes a specialized subroutine LD (\cref{alg.rr}), to handle this scenario. As mentioned, at each iteration, the primary routine, Algorithm~\ref{alg.accelerated utr}, may go through one of two possible paths, determined by the output of the subroutine Algorithm~\ref{alg.rr}.

The first path occurs when the flag $\textrm{ET} = \text{True}$ before the $K_\epsilon$-th iteration of Algorithm~\ref{alg.accelerated utr}, indicating early termination. This happens when the gradient norm continues contracting in the subroutine until a solution $y_k + d_k^{(j)}$ satisfying $\|\nabla f(y_k + d_k^{(j)})\| \leq \epsilon$ is found.

The second path arises if Algorithm~\ref{alg.accelerated utr} is not early terminated within $K_\epsilon$ iterations. In this case, for each $k \in \mathcal{Z}$, the subroutine outputs a step $d_k$ that satisfies the ratio pass (\cref{lem.ratiopass}) and hence the sufficient decrease condition , ensuring that \eqref{eq.es relation} holds throughout these iterations (Roadmap~\ref{fig.standard roadmap}). Consequently, in the end the estimate sequence guarantees us to find a solution $x_k$ such that $f(x_k) - f^* \leq \epsilon$.

% The logic of the analysis is that in Algorithm~\ref{alg.accelerated utr}, if the early terminate occurs before $K_\epsilon = O(\epsilon^{-1/3})$-th iteration, then there is nothing to prove. Otherwise, the estimate sequence technique can guarantee that we find an approximate solution If in $K_\epsilon = O(\epsilon^{-1/3})$ iterations.

We first examine the complexity of Algorithm~\ref{alg.rr} in each iteration $k\in \mathcal{Z}$ of Algorithm~\ref{alg.accelerated utr}, the following lemma shows that either Algorithm~\ref{alg.rr} enters \textbf{Phase 2} in a logarithmic complexity or Algorithm~\ref{alg.accelerated utr} is early terminated.

\begin{lemma}
    \label{lem.inner loop terminate}
    In the $k$-th outer loop of Algorithm~\ref{alg.accelerated utr}, where $k\in\mathcal{Z}$, Algorithm~\ref{alg.rr} will enter \textbf{Phase 2} in
    \begin{equation*}
        j_k \leq O\left (\log \tfrac{\|\nabla f(y_k)\|}{\epsilon}\right)
    \end{equation*}
    iterations, otherwise we find an approximate solution in \textbf{Phase 1} such that
    \begin{equation*}
        \| \nabla f(y_k+d_k^{(j_k)})\| \leq \epsilon.
    \end{equation*}
    % Since the inner loop will terminate eventually, we can actually have a tighter bound for $j_k$:
    % \begin{equation}
    %     \label{eq.tighter bound jk}
    %     j_k \leq O\left (\log \frac{\|\nabla f(y_k)\|}{\|\nabla f(y_k+d_k^{(j_k-1)})\|}\right)
    % \end{equation}
\end{lemma}

When Algorithm~\ref{alg.rr} enters \textbf{Phase 2}, \cref{alg.bisection} is used to find a step that fits ratio pass, that is, $\mu_k = \Theta(\|d_k\|)$. In particular, our choice of parameters in Algorithm~\ref{alg.rr} and Algorithm~\ref{alg.bisection} targets at finding
\begin{equation}\label{eq.goal of bisection 1}
    M\|d_k\| \leq \mu_k\leq 2M\|d_k\|,
\end{equation}
we define the auxiliary function for iteration $k$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq.auxiliary bisection function}
    g_{y_k}(\mu): = \tfrac{\mu}{\|\left(\nabla^2 f(y_k)+\mu I\right)^{-1}\nabla f(y_k)\|}.
\end{equation}
Therefore, the goal \eqref{eq.goal of bisection 1} is equivalent to
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq.goal of bisection 2}
    M\leq g_{y_k}(\mu)\leq 2M.
\end{equation}
The following lemma claims that we have found the bracketing points for the bisection procedure at \textbf{Phase 2}.

\begin{lemma}\label{lem.valid bracketing}
    \label{eq.valid bracketing}
    In the $k$-th iteration, where $k\in\mathcal{Z}$, in \textbf{Phase 2} of Algorithm~\ref{alg.rr},
    \begin{equation*}
        g_{y_k}(\mu_-) < M, \ g_{y_k}(\mu_+) >2M.
    \end{equation*}
\end{lemma}

Also, to guarantee the validity of the bisection search, we establish the following propositions of $g_{y_k}(\mu)$ over the interval $\left[\mu_-,\mu_+\right]$.
\begin{lemma}
    \label{lem.property g}
    For any $k\in\mathcal{Z}$, $g_{y_k}(\mu)$ is continuously differentiable and monotonically increasing for $\mu \in \left (0,+\infty\right ]$. Moreover, its derivative is uniformly bounded above.
\end{lemma}
The above lemmas are crucial in analyzing the complexity of \cref{alg.bisection}. As a result, we have
\begin{lemma}
    \label{lem.bound initial interval}
    At the $k$-th iteration of Algorithm~\ref{alg.accelerated utr}, where $k\in\mathcal{Z}$, the number of linear equations solved in \cref{alg.bisection} is at most $O(\log \frac{\|\nabla f(y_k)\|}{\epsilon})$.
\end{lemma}
As observed, both \textbf{Phase 1} and \textbf{Phase 2} require $O\left(\log\left(\tfrac{\|\nabla f(y_k)\|}{\epsilon}\right)\right)$ iterations; however, we remind readers of the nuances. In \textbf{Phase 1} we need to solve trust-region subproblems \eqref{eq.newTR} while in \textbf{Phase 2} only linear equations are needed. Therefore, the complexity of \textbf{Phase 2} is dominated by the complexity of \textbf{Phase 1}. Also, all these trust-region subproblems and linear equations share the same Hessian and gradient. This means that if we use the eigenvalue decomposition on the Hessian matrix, the decomposition is a one-time effort.

Now it remains to give the following estimate for the gradient norm of the sequence $y_k$ whenever $k\in \mathcal{Z}$. Fortunately, this can be guaranteed by the following lemma.

\begin{lemma}
    \label{lem.upper bound for zero lambda}
    Suppose Assumption~\ref{assm.main assm} and Assumption~\ref{assm.bounded hessian} hold, for $k\in \mathcal{Z}$, we have
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq.upper bound for zero lambda}
        \|\nabla f(y_k)\|\leq \tfrac{9\kappa_H^2}{64M}.
    \end{equation}
    As a consequence, in each iteration of Algorithm~\ref{alg.accelerated utr}, the complexity of Algorithm~\ref{alg.rr} is bounded by $O\left(\log\left(\tfrac{\kappa_H}{M\epsilon}\right)\right)$.
\end{lemma}
Till now we have finished the analysis for the local detection (\cref{alg.rr}). Next, we come back to the auxiliary sequences $A_k$ and $\phi_k(x)$ and show that $x_k,v_k$ updated in both cases of Algorithm~\ref{alg.accelerated utr} satisfies $\mathcal{R}_k^1,\mathcal{R}_k^2$\eqref{eq.es relation}. Our approach is slightly different from the standard one introduced in~\citet{nesterov_lectures_2018}.

We define the recursive equations
\begin{align}
    \label{eq.update a and A}
     & a_k = \tfrac{(k+1)(k+2)}{2}, \ A_k=\tfrac{k(k+1)(k+2)}{6} \\
    \label{eq.update phi}
     & \phi_0(x) = \tfrac{M}{\ell}\|x-x_0\|^3,
\end{align}
For $k\in\mathcal{N}$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq.update phi 1}
    \phi_{k+1}(x) =\phi_k(x)+a_k\left (f(y_k)+\langle \nabla f(y_k),x-y_k\rangle \right),
\end{equation}
For $k\in\mathcal{Z}$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq.update phi 2}
    \phi_{k+1}(x) =\phi_k(x)+a_k\left (f(x_{k+1})+\langle \nabla f(x_{k+1}),x-x_{k+1}\rangle \right ),
\end{equation}

The sequences defined above and the iterates produced by Algorithm~\ref{alg.accelerated utr} satisfy the following propositions.
\begin{lemma}
    \label{lem.property of phi}
    For the function sequence $\phi_k(x)$, they have the following properties
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item $v_k$ is the unique minimizer of $\phi_k(x)$, i.e.,
              \begin{equation*}
                  \phi_k^* =\min \phi_k(x) = \phi_k(v_k).
              \end{equation*}
        \item Suppose Assumption~\ref{assm.main assm} holds, set $\ell = \Theta(1)$, if Algorithm~\ref{alg.accelerated utr} does not early terminate, then $\mathcal{R}_k^1$ and $\mathcal{R}_k^2$ defined in \eqref{eq.es relation 1} and \eqref{eq.es relation 2} hold for all $k\geq0$.
    \end{enumerate}
\end{lemma}

As a direct consequence of the above lemma, the complexity of Algorithm~\ref{alg.accelerated utr} follows,

\begin{theorem}
    \label{thm.convergence outer loop}
    Suppose Assumption~\ref{assm.main assm} and Assumption~\ref{assm.bounded hessian} holds, then Algorithm~\ref{alg.accelerated utr} find an approximate solution satisfying \cref{def.approximate solution} in $K_\epsilon = O(\epsilon^{-1/3})$ iterations. Moreover, the number of the trust-region subproblems solved is bounded by $\tilde O(\epsilon^{-1/3})$.
\end{theorem}
\section{Numerical Experiments}\label{sec.experiments}
In this section, we implement our accelerated Trust-Region method (\atr{}), to compare with the universal trust-region method (\utr{}, \citet{jiang2024nonconvexityuniversaltrustregionmethod}) which targets at convex optimization, the cubic regularization method (\arc{}, \citet{nesterov2006cubic}) and its accelerated version (\arca{}, \citet{nesterov2008accelerating}). We conduct experiments on the Nesterov's worst case function~\cite{nesterovImplementableTensorMethods2021} and the softmax minimization, which are briefly introduced here; further details and the exact experimental setup can be found in Appendix~\ref{sec.experiments.details}. All experiments are conducted on a single machine with a 14-core Apple M4 Pro CPU  and 48GB LPDDR5 RAM.

We begin by testing performance on Nesterov's worst-case function:
\[

where $\eta_{3}(x) = \frac{1}{3}\sum_{i=1}^n |x^{(i)}|^{3}$, $e_1$ denotes the vector with the first coordinate being $1$ and the others being $0$. The problem gets hard as $k$ increases, and $A_k$ is a parametrized bidiagonal matrix (details in \Cref{sec.experiments.nesterov}).

Next, we consider softmax minimization, which approximates piecewise linear functions defined by the data points $a_i \in \mathbb{R}^n,b_i \in \mathbb{R}, i\in 1,...,m$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq.softmax}
    f(x) := \mu \ln \left({\textstyle \sum}_{i=1}^m \exp \left(\tfrac{a_i^Tx - b_i}{\mu}\right)\right).
\end{equation}
The parameter $\mu$ represents the exactness of approximation:
$f(x) \approx \max_{1 \leq i \leq m} \left\{a_i^T x - b_i\right\}.$
This problem is notoriously challenging
due to the large smoothness constant. Generally, smaller $\mu$ produces better approximation, yet the Lipschitz constant $M$ grows larger \cite{doikov_super-universal_2024}.

\section{Conclusion}
In this work, we addressed the challenge of accelerating trust-region-type methods, bridging the gap between their practical success and theoretical development. By proposing a novel framework that effectively utilizes the progress of both function value reduction and gradient norm contraction, we extended the scope of existing acceleration techniques that only supports unconstrained subroutines. Numerical experiments further support our theoretical findings.

\section{Details of Experiments}\label{sec.experiments.details}

We implement \utr{}, \atr{}, \arc{}, and \arca{} in Julia Programming Language. In all algorithms, we have to solve the following canonical subproblem:
\begin{equation}
    \begin{aligned}
        \min_d ~              & \frac{1}{2}d^T\nabla^2 f(x_k)d + \frac{1}{p!}\sigma_k\|d\|^p + \nabla f(x_k)^T d \\
        \operatorname{s.t.} ~ & \|d\| \le r_k.
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where if $p=2$, it is the subproblem in \utr{} and \atr{}; else if $p=3$ and $r_k = +\infty$, it is the subproblem in \arc{} and \arca{}. We implement a unifying procedure to solve the secant equation for step $d_k$, which is exactly \eqref{eq.second-order update}:
\begin{equation}
    d_k = -\left(\nabla^2 f(x_k) + \mu_k I\right)^{-1}\nabla f(x_k)
\end{equation}
where $\mu_k$ is adjusted by standard line search procedure and each linear system is solved via Cholesky factorization. Our implementation is competitive to state-of-the-art solvers in terms of iteration numbers and CPU time. For example, we compare iteration numbers and CPU time in \Cref{fig.softmax} to the implementation in \cite{doikov_super-universal_2024}.

Further details of the test problems are provided in the following.
\paragraph{Nesterov's lower bound problem}\label{sec.experiments.nesterov}
Nesterov's worst-case function for $p$-th order algorithms \cite{nesterovImplementableTensorMethods2021} is defined as the following:
\[
    f_k(x) = \eta_{p+1}\left(A_k x\right) - \left\langle e_1, x\right\rangle, \quad 2 \leq k \leq n,
\]
where $\eta_{p+1}(x) = \tfrac{1}{p+1}\sum_{i=1}^n |x^{(i)}|^{p+1}$, and $A_k$ is a parametrized bidiagonal matrix, and $e_1$ is the standard basis vector with its first coordinate equals $1$.
\small
$$A_k=\left[\begin{array}{cc}U_k & 0 \\ 0 & I_{n-k}\end{array}\right], \quad U_k=\left[\begin{array}{rrccr}1 & -1 & 0 & \ldots & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & -1 & \ldots & 0 \\ & & \ldots & \ldots & \\ 0 & 0 & \ldots & 1 & -1 \\ 0 & 0 & \ldots & 0 & 1\end{array}\right]$$
\normalsize
For a $p$-th order method starting at $x_0 = 0$, the lower bound for $p$-th order algorithm is given by
$O\left(k^{-(1.5p+0.5)}\right)$ (Theorem 4, \cite{nesterovImplementableTensorMethods2021}).
\paragraph{Softmax minimization}
The softmax minimization problem is a smooth approximation of the nonsmooth piecewise linear functions:
$$
    f(x) := \mu \ln \left(\sum_{i=1}^m \exp \left(\tfrac{a_i^Tx - b_i}{\mu}\right)\right) \approx \max_{1 \leq i \leq m} \left\{a_i^T x - b_i\right\} =: \ell(x).
$$
where $i$ is the index of data points, $a_i \in \mathbb{R}^n$ and $b_i \in \mathbb{R}$.
It is a valid approximation because that (see, for example, \cite{beckSmoothingFirstOrder2012})
\begin{align*}
    0 \le  f(x) - \ell(x) \le \mu \log(m), \forall x \in \mathbb{R}^n.
\end{align*}
and the approximation error is bounded by $\mu \log(m)$.
\begin{align*}
    f(x) - \inf f(x) \le \epsilon ~\Rightarrow ~ \ell(x) - \ell(x^*) \le \epsilon + \mu \log(m).
\end{align*}
Choosing a small $\mu$ is hard numerically.
It is known that $f(x)$ has a first-order Lipschitz constant $\tfrac{\|A\|_\infty}{\mu}$. In the experiments, we set $m = 1000, n = 500$, all entries of $a_i, i=1,...,m$ are sampled from the uniform distribution on $[-1,1]$.

\paragraph{Proof to Lemma~\ref{lem.ratiopass}}
\begin{lemma}
    \label{lem.inner product g and d appendix}
    Suppose Assumption~\ref{assm.main assm} holds, and 
    \begin{equation}
    \label{eq.second-order update appendix}
    \left (\nabla^2 f(x) + \mu I \right )d = -\nabla f(x),
    \end{equation}
    holds for some $\mu$ with $\mu\geq M \|d\|$, then we have
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq.inner product g d appendix}
        \langle \nabla f(x+d),-d \rangle \geq \frac{\|\nabla f(x+d)\|^2}{2\mu}+\frac{3}{8}\mu \|d\|^2.
    \end{equation}
    Further, if $\mu \leq 2M\|d\|$, we have
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq.inner product g d advanced appendix}
        \langle \nabla f(x+d),-d \rangle \geq \frac{\sqrt{6}}{6} \frac{\|\nabla f(x+d)\|^{3/2}}{\sqrt{M}}.
    \end{equation}
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
    Set $y=x+d$ in \eqref{eq.first-order exp}, we have
    \begin{equation*}
        \|\nabla f(x+d)-\nabla f(x)-\nabla ^2 f(x) d\| \leq \frac{M}{2}\|d\|^2.
    \end{equation*}
    Plug \eqref{eq.second-order update appendix} into the above, we have
    \begin{equation*}
        \|\nabla f(x+d)+\mu d\| \leq \frac{M}{2}\|d\|^2.
    \end{equation*}
    Squaring both sides and rearranging items, we have
    \begin{equation*}
        \begin{aligned}
            2\mu \langle \nabla f(x+d),-d \rangle & \geq \|\nabla f(x+d)\|^2 +\mu^2 \|d\|^2 -\frac{M^2}{4}\|d\|^4 \\
                                                  & \geq \|\nabla f(x+d)\|^2 +\frac{3}{4}\mu^2 \|d\|^2.
        \end{aligned}
    \end{equation*}
    The second line is due to $\mu\geq M\|d\|$, dividing both sides by $2\mu$ gives \eqref{eq.inner product g d appendix}. Further, when $\mu \leq 2M\|d\|$, the above gives
    \begin{equation*}
        4M\|d\| \langle \nabla f(x+d),-d \rangle\geq 2\mu \langle \nabla f(x+d),-d \rangle\geq \|\nabla f(x+d)\|^2 +\frac{3}{4}\mu^2 \|d\|^2  \geq \|\nabla f(x+d)\|^2 +\frac{3}{4} M^2 \|d\|^4,
    \end{equation*}
    which yields
    \begin{equation*}
        \langle \nabla f(x+d),-d \rangle \geq \frac{\|\nabla f(x+d)\|^2}{4M\|d\|}+\frac{3}{16}M\|d\|^3.
    \end{equation*}
    Consider an auxiliary function $h(t) = \frac{\|\nabla f(x+d)\|^2}{4Mt}+\frac{3}{16}Mt^3$ where $t\geq 0$, taking derivatives gives $h(t)$ achieves its minimum at $t^*= \frac{\sqrt{2}\|\nabla f(x+d)\|^{1/2}}{\sqrt{3M}}$, plugging $t^*$ back gives \eqref{eq.inner product g d advanced appendix}.
\end{proof}

\paragraph{Proof to the Complexity of the Bisection Procedure}
Since both Lemma~\ref{lem.valid bracketing}-\ref{lem.bound initial interval} are intermediate, we proof them altogether to derive the complexity of the bisection procedure.

Suppose in the $k$-th iteration of Algorithm~\ref{alg.accelerated utr}, the Local detection subroutine Algorithm~\ref{alg.rr} enters the bisection procedure at the $j_k$-th inner iteration, then by the mechanism of Algorithm~\ref{alg.rr}, the following can be established:
\begin{gather}
    \label{eq.magnitude lambda}
    \lambda_k^{(j_k)} > \frac{\sqrt{M}}{3}\|\nabla f(y_k+d_k^{(j_k-1)})\|^{1/2}\\
    \label{eq.set mu plus}
    \mu_+ = \lambda_k^{(j_k)}+\frac{\sqrt{M}}{3}\|\nabla f(y_k+d_k^{(j_k-1)})\|^{1/2}> \frac{2\sqrt{M}}{3}\|\nabla f(y_k+d_k^{(j_k-1)})\|^{1/2}\\
    \label{eq.newton equation}
    \left (\nabla ^2 f(y_k)+\mu_+ I\right) d_k^{(j_k)}=-\nabla f(y_k)\\
    \label{eq.step size}
    \|d_k^{(j_k)}\| = \frac{1}{3\sqrt{M}}\|\nabla f(y_k+d_k^{(j_k-1)})\|^{1/2}.
\end{gather}

We first review the mission of the bisection procedure: for the auxiliary function $g_{y_k}(\mu) = \frac{\mu}{\|\left (\nabla^2 f(y_k)+\mu I\right)^{-1}\nabla f(y_k)\|}$, given current point $y_k$, our goal is to find the regularize $\mu$ such that
\begin{equation*}
    M \leq g_{y_k}(\mu) \leq 2M.
\end{equation*}
We now show that the choices $\mu_+,\mu_-$ in Algorithm~\ref{alg.rr} are valid for the bisection procedure,
\begin{lemma}\label{lem.valid bracketing appendix}
    \label{eq.valid bracketing appendix}
    In the $k$-th iteration, where $k\in\mathcal Z$, in \textbf{Phase 2} of Algorithm~\ref{alg.rr},
    \begin{equation*}
        g_{y_k}(\mu_-) < M, \ g_{y_k}(\mu_+) >2M.
    \end{equation*}
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
    From the mechanism \eqref{eq.magnitude lambda}-\eqref{eq.step size} of Algorithm~\ref{alg.rr}, we have
    \begin{equation*}
        \| \left (\nabla ^2 f(y_k)+\mu^+ I\right )^{-1} \nabla f(y_k)\| = \frac{1}{3\sqrt{M}}\|\nabla f(y_k+d_k^{(j_k-1)})\|^{1/2}, \ \mu_+ >\frac{2\sqrt{M}}{3}\|\nabla f(y_k+d_k^{(j_k-1)})\|^{1/2}=2M\|d_k^{(j_k)}\|,
    \end{equation*}
    thus $g_{y_k}(\mu_+) >2M$. Since $\mu_- <\mu^+$, then
    \begin{equation*}
        \| \left (\nabla ^2 f(y_k)+\mu^- I\right )^{-1} \nabla f(y_k)\|>\| \left (\nabla ^2 f(y_k)+\mu^+ I\right )^{-1} \nabla f(y_k)\| = \frac{1}{3\sqrt{M}}\|\nabla f(y_k+d_k^{(j_k-1)})\|^{1/2},
    \end{equation*}
    therefore $g_{y_k}(\mu_-)< M$.
\end{proof}

Then we analyze some basic properties of $g_{y_k}(\mu)$,
\begin{lemma}
    \label{lem.property g appendix}
    For any $k\in\mathcal{Z}$, $g_{y_k}(\mu)$ is continuously differentiable and monotonically increasing for $\mu \in \left (0,+\infty\right ]$ and
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq.derivative}
        g_{y_k}'(\mu) = \frac{1}{\|\left (\nabla ^2 f(y_k)+\mu I \right)^{-1}\nabla f(y_k)\|}+\frac{\mu}{\|\left (\nabla ^2 f(y_k)+\mu I \right)^{-1}\nabla f(y_k)\|^3}\sum_{i=1}^n\frac{\beta_i^2}{\left (\lambda_i+\mu\right )^3},
    \end{equation}
    and it is bounded both below and above
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq.derivative bound}
        0< g_{y_k}'(\mu) \leq \frac{2}{\|\left (\nabla ^2 f(y_k)+\mu I \right)^{-1}\nabla f(y_k)\|}.
    \end{equation}
    Where $\lambda_i$ is the $i$-th eigenvalue of $\nabla ^2 f(y_k)$, $\beta_i = \nabla f(y_k)^T v_i$, $v_i$ denotes the eigenvector corresponding to $\lambda_i$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
    Since $\nabla^2 f(y_k)+\mu I \succ 0$ for $\mu>0$, we can apply eigen decomposition to it, then
    \begin{equation*}
        \|\left (\nabla ^2 f(y_k)+\mu I \right)^{-1}\nabla f(y_k)\|=\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n \frac{\beta_i^2}{\left (\lambda_i+\mu\right )^2}}, \ g_{y_k}(\mu) = \frac{\mu}{\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n \frac{\beta_i^2}{\left (\lambda_i+\mu\right )^2}}}.
    \end{equation*}
    Obviously $g_{y_k}(\mu)$ is increasing in $\mu \in \left (0,+\infty\right ]$. Also, through some basic calculations, we have
    \begin{equation*}
        \begin{aligned}
            g_{y_k}'(\mu) & = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n \frac{\beta_i^2}{\left (\lambda_i+\mu\right )^2}}}+\mu \frac{1}{\left ( (\sum_{i=1}^n \frac{\beta_i^2}{\left (\lambda_i+\mu\right )^2}\right )^{3/2}}\sum_{i=1}^n\frac{\beta_i^2}{\left (\lambda_i+\mu\right )^3} \\
                          & = \frac{1}{\|\left (\nabla ^2 f(y_k)+\mu I \right)^{-1}\nabla f(y_k)\|}+\frac{\mu}{\|\left (\nabla ^2 f(y_k)+\mu I \right)^{-1}\nabla f(y_k)\|^3}\sum_{i=1}^n\frac{\beta_i^2}{\left (\lambda_i+\mu\right )^3}                                   \\
                          & \leq \frac{1}{\|\left (\nabla ^2 f(y_k)+\mu I \right)^{-1}\nabla f(y_k)\|}+\frac{\mu}{\|\left (\nabla ^2 f(y_k)+\mu I \right)^{-1}\nabla f(y_k)\|^3}\sum_{i=1}^n\frac{\beta_i^2}{\mu\left (\lambda_i+\mu\right )^2}                             \\
                          & =  \frac{2}{\|\left (\nabla ^2 f(y_k)+\mu I \right)^{-1}\nabla f(y_k)\|}.
        \end{aligned}
    \end{equation*}
    The inequality is because of $\lambda_i \geq 0$ for $i=1,\ldots,n$.
\end{proof}

From the continuity and monotonicity of $g_{y_k}(\mu)$, we know there exist $\mu_-<\mu_l<\mu_u<\mu_+$ such that
\begin{equation}\label{eq.target interval bracketing points}
    g_{y_k}(\mu_l)=M, \ g_{y_k}(\mu_u)=2M.
\end{equation}
To clarify, now we are given the initial interval $[\mu_-,\mu_+]$ and are using bisection to find some $\mu\in[\mu_l,\mu_u]$, once we establish a lower bound for the length of the target interval then we can bound the complexity for the bisection procedure.
\begin{lemma}
    \label{lem.bound target interval}
    For $\mu_l,\mu_u$ in \eqref{eq.target interval bracketing points}, we have
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq.bound target interval}
        \mu_u-\mu_l \geq \frac{\sqrt{M}\|\nabla f(y_k+d_k^{(j_k-1)})\|^{1/2}}{6}.
    \end{equation}
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
    By the mean value theorem, there exists $\xi\in \left [\mu_l,\mu_u\right]$, such that
    \begin{equation*}
        M=g_{y_k}(\mu_u)-g_{y_k}(\mu_l) = g_{y_k}'(\xi)(\mu_u-\mu_l),
    \end{equation*}
    combine the above with \eqref{eq.derivative bound} and \eqref{eq.target interval bracketing points} we have
    \begin{equation*}
        \begin{aligned}
            \mu_+-\mu_- & =  \frac{1}{g_{y_k}'(\xi)}\left(g_{y_k}(\mu_u)-g_{y_k}(\mu_l)\right)           \\
                        & \geq \frac{M\|\left (\nabla ^2 f(y_k)+\xi I \right )^{-1}\nabla f(y_k)\|}{2}   \\
                        & \geq \frac{M\|\left (\nabla ^2 f(y_k)+\mu_+ I \right )^{-1}\nabla f(y_k)\|}{2} \\
                        & = \frac{\sqrt{M}\|\nabla f(y_k+d_k^{(j_k-1)})\|^{1/2}}{6}.
        \end{aligned}
    \end{equation*}
    The third line is because of $\mu_+ >\xi$, the last line is from \eqref{eq.newton equation} and \eqref{eq.step size}.
\end{proof}
Now we established a lower bound for the target interval, it remains to bound the length of the initial interval $\mu_+ -\mu_- = \lambda_k^{(j_k)}$.
\begin{lemma}
    \label{lem.bound initial interval appendix}
    At the $k$-th iteration of Algorithm~\ref{alg.accelerated utr}, if the Algorithm~\ref{alg.rr} enters \textbf{Phase 2}, we have
    \begin{equation*}
        \lambda_k^{(j_k)}\leq \frac{3\sqrt{M}\|\nabla f(y_k)\|}{\|\nabla f(y_k+d_k^{(j_k-1)})\|^{1/2}}\leq O\left(\log\frac{\|\nabla f(y_k)\|}{\epsilon}\right).
    \end{equation*}
    As a consequence, the complexity of the bisection search procedure is
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq.complexity bisection}
        O \left (\log \left( \frac{\mu_+-\mu_-}{\mu_u-\mu_l}\right)\right)= O\left (\log \left(\frac{\|\nabla f(y_k)\|}{\|\nabla f(y_k+d_k^{(j_k-1)})\|}\right)\right)
    \end{equation}
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
    Note that by the mechanism of Algorithm~\ref{alg.rr}, when bisection begins, we have
    \begin{equation*}
        \|\left (\nabla^2 f(y_k)+\mu_+ I \right)d_k^{(j_k)}\| =\|\nabla f(y_k)\|, \ \|d_k^{(j_k)}\| =\frac{1}{3\sqrt{M}}\|\nabla f(y_k+d_k^{(j_k-1)})\|^{1/2}.
    \end{equation*}
    Note that $\nabla ^2 f(y_k)\succeq 0$, and $\mu_+ \geq \lambda_k^{(j_k)}$, we have
    \begin{equation*}
        \lambda_k^{(j_k)}\|d_k^{(j_k)}\| \leq \|\nabla f(y_k)\|,
    \end{equation*}
    thus
    \begin{equation*}
        \lambda_k^{(j_k)} \leq \frac{\|\nabla f(y_k)\|}{\|d_k^{(j_k)}\|} = \frac{3\sqrt{M}\|\nabla f(y_k)\|}{\|\nabla f(y_k+d_k^{(j_k-1)})\|^{1/2}}.
    \end{equation*}
    Combine the above with \eqref{eq.bound target interval} we have
    \begin{equation*}
        \frac{\mu_+-\mu_-}{\mu_u-\mu_l} \leq \frac{18\|\nabla f(y_k)\|}{\|\nabla f(y_k+d_k^{(j_k-1)})\|}.
    \end{equation*}
    Note that $\|\nabla f(y_k+d_k^{(j_k-1)})\|\geq \epsilon$. Hence the proof is finished.
\end{proof}
\paragraph{Proof to Lemma~\ref{lem.upper bound for zero lambda}}
\begin{lemma}
    \label{lem.upper bound for zero lambda appendix}
    Suppose Assumption~\ref{assm.main assm} and Assumption~\ref{assm.bounded hessian} hold, for $k\in \mathcal{Z}$, we have
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq.upper bound for zero lambda appendix}
        \|\nabla f(y_k)\|\leq \frac{9\kappa_H^2}{64M}.
    \end{equation}
    As a consequence, in each iteration of Algorithm~\ref{alg.accelerated utr}, the complexity of Algorithm~\ref{alg.rr} is bounded by $O\left(\log\left(\frac{\kappa_H}{M\epsilon}\right)\right)$
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
    Note that when $k\in \mathcal{Z}$, we have $\lambda_k =0$, hence the following hold when it enters the local detection phase
    \begin{equation*}
        \left (\nabla^2 f(y_k)+\frac{\sqrt{M}}{3}\|\nabla f(y_k)\|^{1/2} I\right )d_k^{(-1)} = -\nabla f(y_k), \ \|d_k^{(-1)}\| \leq \frac{1}{3\sqrt{M}}\|\nabla f(y_k)\|^{1/2}.
    \end{equation*}
    Therefore, by triangle inequality, it holds that
    \begin{equation*}
        \|\nabla f(y_k)\| \leq \|\nabla^2 f(y_k) d_k^{(-1)}\| +\frac{\sqrt{M}}{3}\|\nabla f(y_k)\|^{1/2}\|d_k^{(-1)}\| \leq \|\nabla^2 f(y_k) d_k^{(-1)}\|+\frac{1}{9}\|\nabla f(y_k)\|.
    \end{equation*}
    By the above inequality and Assumption~\ref{assm.bounded hessian}, we have
    \begin{equation*}
        \begin{aligned}
        \frac{8}{9}\|\nabla f(y_k)\|&\leq \|\nabla^2 f(y_k) d_k^{(-1)}\|\\
        &\leq \kappa_H \|d_k^{(-1)}\|\\
        &\leq \kappa_H \cdot \frac{1}{3\sqrt{M}}\|\nabla f(y_k)\|^{1/2} .
        \end{aligned}
    \end{equation*}
    Rearrange items we will have \eqref{eq.upper bound for zero lambda appendix}.
\end{proof}
\paragraph{Proof to the Total Complexity of Algorithm~\ref{alg.accelerated utr}}
Since the iteration complexity results of Theorem~\ref{thm.convergence outer loop} is a direct consequence of Lemma~\ref{lem.property of phi} and the number of total subproblems solved is a direct consequence of the above Lemma~\ref{lem.inner loop terminate} and the complexity of the bisection search, we will focus on the proof of Lemma~\ref{lem.property of phi}.

Here are some basic properties of the estimate sequence, which is modified from \citet{nesterov_lectures_2018}.
\begin{lemma}
    \label{lem.magnitude a and A}
    For $k\geq 0$, we have
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq.magnitude a and A}
        \frac{A_{k+1}}{a_k^{3/2}} \geq \frac{\sqrt{2}}{3}.
    \end{equation}
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
    $\frac{A_{k+1}}{a_k^{3/2}}= \frac{(k+1)(k+2)(k+3)}{6}\cdot \frac{2^{3/2}}{(k+1)^{3/2}(k+2)^{3/2}} = \frac{\sqrt{2}(k+3)}{3(k+1)^{1/2}(k+2)^{1/2}} \geq \frac{\sqrt{2}}{3}.$
\end{proof}
We call a differentiable function $d(x)$ on $\mathbb{R}^n$ uniformly convex \citet[Section 4.2.2]{nesterov_lectures_2018} of degree $p\geq 2$ with constant $q>0$ if
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq.uniformly convex of p}
    d(y)\geq d(x)+\langle \nabla d(x),y-x\rangle +\frac{q}{p}\|y-x\|^p, \ \forall x,y\in\mathbb{R}^n.
\end{equation}
\begin{lemma}
    \label{lem.property of phi appendix}
    For the function sequence $\phi_k(x)$, they have the following properties
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item $\phi_k(x)$ is uniformly convex of degree $3$ with constant $\frac{3M}{2\ell}$.
        \item $v_k$ is the unique minimizer of $\phi_k(x)$, and
              \begin{equation}
                  \label{eq.phi_k phi_low}
                  \phi_k(x)\geq \phi_k^* + \frac{M}{2\ell}\|x-v_k\|^3.,
              \end{equation}
              where $\phi_k^* =\min \phi_k(x).$
    \end{enumerate}
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
    The first claim is from the definition of $\phi_0$ in \eqref{eq.update phi}, for analysis of uniform convex functions, please refer to \citet[Section 4.2.2]{nesterov_lectures_2018}. For the second claim, we have
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq.phi recursive}
        \phi_k(x) = \phi_0(x)+\sum_{i\in \mathcal{N},i<k}a_i\left (f(y_i)+\langle \nabla f(y_i),x-y_i\rangle \right)+\sum_{j\in \mathcal{Z},j<k}a_j\left (f(x_{j+1})+\langle \nabla f(x_{j+1}),x-x_{j+1}\rangle \right).
    \end{equation}
    From optimality condition
    \begin{equation*}
        \begin{aligned}
            \nabla \phi_k(x) & = \nabla \phi_0(x)+\sum_{i\in \mathcal{N},i<k}a_i\nabla f(y_i)+ \sum_{j\in \mathcal{Z},j<k}a_j\nabla f(x_{j+1})                \\
                             & = \frac{3M}{\ell}\|x-x_0\|(x-x_0)+\sum_{i\in \mathcal{N},i<k}a_i\nabla f(y_i)+ \sum_{j\in \mathcal{Z},j<k}a_j\nabla f(x_{j+1}) \\
                             & =0.
        \end{aligned}
    \end{equation*}
    Solving the optimality condition and noticing the way we update $s_k$ gives
    \begin{equation*}
        x = v_0 - \sqrt{\frac{\ell}{3M\|s_{k}\|}}s_{k},
    \end{equation*}
    which is $v_k$. Setting $d(x)=\phi_k(x)$, $q=\frac{3M}{2\ell}$ in \eqref{eq.uniformly convex of p} gives \eqref{eq.phi_k phi_low}.
\end{proof}
During the update of the algorithm, we can show that the following properties hold
\begin{lemma}\label{lem.estimate sequence}
    If we set $0<\ell \leq\frac{25}{12\cdot 54^2}$, then we can guarantee the following for all $k\geq 0$:
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq.estimate sequence relation}
        A_k f(x_k)\leq \phi_k^*  \leq \phi_k(x)\leq A_k f(x)+\phi_0(x), \ \forall x\in \mathbb{R}^n.
    \end{equation}
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
    We prove by induction, note that $A_0 = 0$, so \eqref{eq.estimate sequence relation} holds for $i=0$. Suppose it also holds for $i=k$,
    We first check
    $$\phi_{k+1}(x)\leq A_{k+1} f(x)+\phi_0(x),$$
    suppose $k\in \mathcal{N}$
    \begin{equation*}
        \begin{aligned}
            \phi_{k+1}(x) & =\phi_k(x)+a_k\left (f(y_k)+\langle \nabla f(y_k),x-y_k\rangle \right)               \\
                          & \leq A_k f(x)+\phi_0(x) +a_k\left (f(y_k)+\langle \nabla f(y_k),x-y_k\rangle \right) \\
                          & \leq A_k f(x)+\phi_0(x) +a_k f(x)                                                    \\
                          & =A_{k+1} f(x)+\phi_0(x).
        \end{aligned}
    \end{equation*}
    The first inequality is from the induction hypothesis. The proof for the case $k\in \mathcal{Z}$ is exactly the same.

    We now check $\phi_{k+1}^* \geq A_{k+1}f(x_{k+1})$, in the following inequalities, $z_k=y_k$ if $k\in\mathcal{N}$ and $z_k=x_{k+1}$ if $k\in\mathcal{Z}$
    \begin{equation*}
        \begin{aligned}
            \phi_{k+1}^* & = \min \left \{ \phi_k(x)+a_k \left (f(z_k)+\langle \nabla f(z_k),x-z_k\rangle \right)\right \}                                                                                                    \\
                         & \geq \min \left \{ \phi_k^* +\frac{M}{2\ell}\|x-v_k\|^3+a_k \left (f(z_k)+\langle \nabla f(z_k),x-z_k\rangle \right)\right \}                                                                      \\
                         & \geq \min \left \{ A_k f(x_k) +\frac{M}{2\ell}\|x-v_k\|^3+a_k \left (f(z_k)+\langle \nabla f(z_k),x-z_k\rangle \right)\right \}                                                                    \\
                         & \geq \min \left \{ A_k f(z_k) +A_k \langle \nabla f(z_k),x_k-z_k\rangle +\frac{M}{2\ell}\|x-v_k\|^3+a_k \left (f(z_k)+\langle \nabla f(z_k),x-z_k\rangle \right)\right \}                          \\
                         & = \min \left \{ A_{k+1} f(z_k)+A_{k+1} \langle \nabla f(z_k),\frac{A_k}{A_{k+1}}x_k+\frac{a_k}{A_{k+1}}v_k-z_k\rangle +\frac{M}{2\ell}\|x-v_k\|^3+a_k\langle \nabla f(z_k),x-v_k\rangle\right \}   \\
                         & = A_{k+1} f(z_k)+A_{k+1} \langle \nabla f(z_k),\frac{A_k}{A_{k+1}}x_k+\frac{a_k}{A_{k+1}}v_k-z_k\rangle-\frac{2\sqrt{2}\sqrt{\ell}\cdot a_k^{3/2}}{3\sqrt{3}\cdot\sqrt{M}}\|\nabla f(z_k)\|^{3/2}.
        \end{aligned}
    \end{equation*}
    In the above analysis, the first line is because of the uniform convexity of $\phi_k$, the second line is from the induction hypothesis, and the third line is from the convexity of $f$. When $k\in \mathcal{N}$, we have
    \begin{equation*}
        \begin{aligned}
            \phi_{k+1}^* & \geq A_{k+1}f(y_k)-\frac{2\sqrt{2}\sqrt{\ell}\cdot a_k^{3/2}}{3\sqrt{3}\cdot\sqrt{M}}\|\nabla f(y_k)\|^{3/2} \\
                         & = A_{k+1}\left(f(y_k)-A_{k+1}^{-1}\frac{2\sqrt{2}\sqrt{\ell}\cdot a_k^{3/2}}{3\sqrt{3}\cdot\sqrt{M}}\|\nabla f(y_k)\|^{3/2}\right)\\
                         & \geq A_{k+1}f(x_{k+1}).
        \end{aligned}
    \end{equation*}
    The second inequality is from the way we set $\ell$, \eqref{eq.decrease of order 1.5 appendix}, and \eqref{eq.magnitude a and A}.

    When $k\in \mathcal{Z}$, we have
    \begin{equation*}
        \begin{aligned}
            \phi_{k+1}^* & \geq A_{k+1} f(x_{k+1}) +A_{k+1}\langle \nabla f(x_{k+1}),y_k-x_{k+1} \rangle -\frac{2\sqrt{2}\sqrt{\ell}\cdot a_k^{3/2}}{3\sqrt{3}\cdot\sqrt{M}}\|\nabla f(x_{k+1})\|^{3/2}                         \\
                         & = A_{k+1} f(x_{k+1}) +A_{k+1}\left (\langle \nabla f(x_{k+1}),y_k-x_{k+1} \rangle -A_{k+1}^{-1}\frac{2\sqrt{2}\sqrt{\ell}\cdot a_k^{3/2}}{3\sqrt{3}\cdot\sqrt{M}}\|\nabla f(x_{k+1})\|^{3/2}\right ) \\
                         & \geq A_{k+1} f(x_{k+1}) +A_{k+1}\left (\langle \nabla f(x_{k+1}),y_k-x_{k+1} \rangle -\frac{2\sqrt{\ell}}{\sqrt{3}\cdot\sqrt{M}}\|\nabla f(x_{k+1})\|^{3/2}\right ).
        \end{aligned}
    \end{equation*}
    The last inequality is from \eqref{eq.magnitude a and A}. Now it reduces to prove
    \begin{equation*}
        \langle f(x_{k+1}),y_k-x_{k+1} \rangle -\frac{2\sqrt{\ell}}{\sqrt{3}\cdot\sqrt{M}}\|\nabla f(x_{k+1})\|^{3/2} \geq 0.
    \end{equation*}
    Note that the output of Algorithm~\ref{alg.rr} satisfies
    \begin{gather*}
        x_{k+1}=y_k+d_k\\
        \left (\nabla ^2 f(y_k)+\mu_k I\right)d_k = -\nabla f(y_k)\\
        M\|d_k\| \leq \mu_k \leq 2M\|d_k\|.
    \end{gather*}
    As a result of \eqref{eq.inner product g d advanced appendix}, we have
    \begin{equation*}
        \langle f(x_{k+1}),y_k-x_{k+1} \rangle -\frac{2\sqrt{\ell}}{\sqrt{3}\cdot\sqrt{M}}\|\nabla f(x_{k+1})\|^{3/2} \geq \frac{\sqrt{6}}{6\sqrt{M}}\|\nabla f(x_{k+1})\|^{3/2}-\frac{2\sqrt{\ell}}{\sqrt{3}\cdot\sqrt{M}}\|\nabla f(x_{k+1})\|^{3/2} \geq 0.
    \end{equation*}
    Thus the proof is finished.
\end{proof}
Till now we have finished proof for the recursive relation in the estimate sequence technique. The $O(\epsilon^{-1/3})$ iteration complexity is a consequence of setting $x=x^*$ in \eqref{eq.estimate sequence relation} and the fact $A_k = \Omega(k^3)$.
