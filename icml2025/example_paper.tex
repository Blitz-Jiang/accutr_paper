\begin{abstract}
    Trust-Region-type method is a fundamental class of optimization algorithms, but the constrained nature of its subproblem has made complexity analysis particularly challenging, especially in the acceleration scenario. In this paper, we propose the first accelerated trust-region-type algorithm and establish improved global complexity of $\tilde O(\epsilon^{-1/3})$, addressing a longstanding challenge in the field. Numerical experiments demonstrate that the proposed algorithm achieves performance comparable to Nesterov's accelerated cubic Newton method, while consistently outperforming standard non-accelerated trust-region and cubic Newton methods. Our work contributes to the theoretical foundation of the trust-region-type methods, offering insights that may inspire future research in this area.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{Intro}
The trust-region (TR) method \cite{More1983,conn2000trust} is recognized as a powerful second-order method due to its effectiveness in solving practical problems and its significant impact across various fields. It has been successfully applied in machine learning applications, such as adversarial neural networks \cite{yao2019trust}, real-time tracking \cite{liu2004real}, and mixed norm regression \cite{kim2010scalable}. Notably, the TR method has also inspired the widely used trust-region policy optimization \cite{schulman2015trust, kurutach2018model} in the reinforcement learning community.

In this paper, we consider the unconstrained optimization problem \begin{equation} \label{eq.main problem} \min_{x\in \mathbb{R}^n}~ f(x), \end{equation} where $f:\mathbb{R}^n\to \mathbb{R}$ satisfies Assumption~\ref{assm.main assm}. The standard TR method operates on the local quadratic approximation of the objective function within a TR ball, solving the subproblem:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq.classic tr subproblem} \begin{aligned} \min_{d \in \mathbb{R}^n} \quad &m_k(d):= \nabla f(x_k)^T d+\frac{1}{2}d^T \nabla^2 f(x_k) d, \\ \operatorname{s.t.} \quad &\|d\|\le \Delta_k. \end{aligned}
\end{equation}


While it has demonstrated outstanding success in practice, its theoretical underpinnings are still evolving, and complexity analyses have proven challenging.

Historically, the convergence analyses of the standard TR method were primarily asymptotic, offering little insight into global complexity. Foundational results can be found in the textbook~\cite{conn2000trust, cartis2022evaluation} and the review~\cite{yuan2015recent}.

Recently, global complexity has emerged as a crucial metric for evaluating optimization algorithms. Early studies established that the standard TR method converges to stationary points with a complexity of $O(\epsilon^{-2})$, matching that of first-order methods but being suboptimal in terms of second-order methods. Many researchers have struggled to establish satisfactory complexity analysis for the standard TR method, which has led to the proposal of various TR-type variants to achieve global complexity results.

Much of their complexity analysis has focused on finding approximate stationary points of nonconvex functions. Numerous TR-type variants that are capable of finding $\epsilon$-approximate solutions with complexity of $O(\epsilon^{-3/2})$ have been proposed~\cite{luenberger_linear_2021, curtis2017trust, curtis2021trust, hamad2022consistently, hamad2024simplepracticaladaptivetrustregion}. While their global complexity results are similar, these methods differ in algorithmic design and analytical techniques. For details on their approaches, we refer readers to their respective works. For convex optimization, \cite{jiang2024nonconvexityuniversaltrustregionmethod} proposed a TR variant with a complexity of $O(\epsilon^{-1/2})$ very recently.

We acknowledge the significant contributions of the above results, including fundamental complexity analyses and innovative algorithmic mechanisms. However, there remains considerable room for improvement. Compared to TR-type methods, another milestone second-order approach, the cubic regularized Newton method~\cite{nesterov2006cubic}, demonstrates greater promise in convex optimization due to its ability to be accelerated~\cite{nesterov2008accelerating}. Specifically, while achieving complexities of $O(\epsilon^{-3/2})$ for nonconvex problems and $O(\epsilon^{-1/2})$ for convex problems, it can be further accelerated to $O(\epsilon^{-1/3})$ in the convex setting~\cite{nesterov2008accelerating}. This marks a stark contrast to the TR method's current limitations.

The pursuit of acceleration techniques~\cite{nesterov_lectures_2018} to enhance optimization algorithms has emerged as a critical research focus, driven by the theoretical and practical successes of accelerated first-order methods in machine learning~\cite{lan_first-order_2020}. This growing interest extends to accelerating second-order methods, offering both improved performance guarantees and practical utility.

Beyond the accelerated cubic Newton method, other accelerated second-order approaches have also been developed~\cite{monteiro2013accelerated,carmon2022optimal,doikov2020contracting}. However, all the above methods deal with unconstrained subproblems, whereas the trust-region method, with its inherently constrained subproblems, remains notably absent from this landscape.

Building on the above existing results and observation, a natural question arises: \emph{Can the TR-type method be further accelerated?}

Addressing this question is pivotal for advancing the theoretical understanding of TR methods and unlocking their full potential in convex optimization settings. However, this goal presents significant challenges arising from the ball constraint.

First, the constrained TR subproblem inevitably introduces posterior multipliers, which bring intricate dependencies among the step size, multiplier, and gradient norm. Bridging these factors requires more sophisticated mechanisms and detailed analyses.

In contrast, existing accelerated algorithms all have unconstrained subproblems, which approximate the objective function with Taylor expansions. For example, both gradient descent and cubic Newton methods naturally fit into this form. These unconstrained subproblems have the advantage of avoiding explicit multipliers, with step sizes scaling naturally as a polynomial of the gradient norm. Consequently, the per-iteration progress is easier to analyze.

Second, accelerating second-order methods heavily relies on the estimating sequence technique~\cite{nesterov_lectures_2018}, which establishes a recursive relationship across iterations to ensure that, cumulatively, the algorithm achieves sufficient progress in function value. For unconstrained subproblems, this relationship is often adequate, as the updates typically ensure a sufficient decrease. However, when applied to TR-type methods, this approach faces additional challenges. The constrained nature of TR subproblems means that function value progress is not guaranteed at every iteration. Instead, each iteration may either result in a reduction in the function value or a reduction in the gradient norm, with these two types of progress alternating across iterations. As a result, the standard estimating sequence technique, which primarily focuses on function value reduction, is not directly applicable to TR-type methods. To address this, the technique must be customized to account for both function value and gradient norm reductions throughout the algorithm.

As far as we know, the only literature that considers accelerating the constrained subproblem is~\cite{carmon2020acceleration}. They accelerate the ball subproblem of the form
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq.ball oracle}
    \begin{aligned} \min_{x \in \mathbb{R}^n} \quad &f(x), \\ \operatorname{s.t.} \quad &\|x-x_k\|\le r. \end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Their algorithm exhibited an acceleration from $O\left(R/r\right)$ to $O\left(\left(R/r\right)^{2/3}\right)$, where $R:= \|x_0-x^*\|$. We remark that their complexity results focus on $r$, which diverges from our goal.

\paragraph{Contribution} In this paper, we design the first accelerated TR-type method that achieves a global complexity of $\Tilde{O}(\epsilon^{-1/3})$ for finding $\epsilon$-approximate solutions in convex optimization.

Technically, we introduce a more flexible TR-type subproblem that effectively controls both the function value and the gradient norm in subsequent iterations. Unlike existing accelerated second-order methods, which primarily focus on function value decrease, our approach also incorporates the reduction of the gradient norm, which aligns more closely with the specific characteristics of TR-type methods.

Numerical experiments demonstrate the efficacy of our method. It is comparable to the accelerated cubic Newton method~\cite{nesterov2008accelerating} and outperforms the non-accelerated counterparts of both the TR method~\cite{jiang2024nonconvexityuniversaltrustregionmethod} and the accelerated cubic Newton method~\cite{nesterov2006cubic}.

\paragraph{Related Works}
Here we review the literature on accelerated second-order methods. Apart from the accelerated cubic Newton method, another significant development in the accelerated second-order method is the MS acceleration framework~\cite{monteiro2013accelerated}, which serves as a general approach for incorporating second-order methods as inner oracles, achieving a complexity of $\tilde{O}(\epsilon^{-2/7})$. \citet{carmon2022optimal} further demonstrated that some second-order methods qualify as oracles within the MS framework.

More recently, a new second-order method employing gradient regularization was introduced by~\citet{mishchenko_regularized_2023}. This approach can be accelerated using the contracting mapping technique~\cite{doikov2020contracting}, leading to the development of the accelerated gradient regularized Newton method, which achieves a global complexity of $\tilde{O}(\epsilon^{-1/3})$.

\section{Preliminaries}
\label{priliminary}
Let $\|\cdot\|$ denote the standard Euclidean norm in $\mathbb{R}^n$, and let $x^T y$ represent the inner product between $x, y \in \mathbb{R}^n$. For a matrix $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$, $\|A\|$ denotes the induced $\ell_2$ norm, and $\lambda_{\max}(A)$ refers to its largest eigenvalue. We use the standard asymptotic notations $O, \Omega, \Theta$ in their conventional sense, where $\tilde{O}$ hides logarithmic terms within $O$. Specifically, for two constants $A$ and $B$, we say that $A = O(B)$ if there exists a constant $c > 0$ such that $A \leq c \cdot B$, and $A = \Omega(B)$ if there exists a constant $c > 0$ such that $A \geq c \cdot B$. We write $A = \Theta(B)$ if both $A = O(B)$ and $A = \Omega(B)$ hold.

Throughout this paper, we focus on convex objective functions and assume the following conditions:

\begin{assumption}
    \label{assm.main assm}
    The objective function $f: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ is twice continuously differentiable, and its Hessian is Lipschitz continuous. That is, there exists a constant $M > 0$ such that for all $x, y \in \mathbb{R}^n$,
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq.main assm}
        \|\nabla^2 f(x) - \nabla^2 f(y)\| \leq M \|x - y\|. \end{equation}
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}
    \label{assm.bounded hessian}
    The Hessian of the objective function is bounded. Specifically, there exists a constant $\kappa_H > 0$ such that for all $x \in \mathbb{R}^n$, \begin{equation}
        \label{eq.bounded hessian}
        \|\nabla^2 f(x)\| \leq \kappa_H.
    \end{equation} \end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}
    \label{assm.solvable}
    There exists a minimizer $x^* \in \mathbb{R}^n$ such that
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq.solvable}
        f(x^*) = f^* := \min_{x \in \mathbb{R}^n} f(x).
    \end{equation}
\end{assumption}

We are interested in finding $\epsilon$-approximate solutions, as defined below:

\begin{definition}
    \label{def.approximate solution}
    Given $\epsilon > 0$, a point $x \in \mathbb{R}^n$ is an $\epsilon$-approximate solution to problem~\eqref{eq.main problem} if
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq.approximate solution}
        f(x) - f^* \leq \epsilon \quad \text{or} \quad \|\nabla f(x)\| \leq \epsilon.
    \end{equation}
\end{definition}

To find such approximate solutions, we focus on accelerating the TR method. Specifically, we propose a modification to the standard TR subproblem to improve its theoretical results. Unlike the classical TR subproblem~\eqref{eq.classic tr subproblem}, we introduce a new form of the subproblem at the current iterate $x_k$.

The modified TR subproblem is given by:
\begin{equation}\label{eq.newTR}
    \begin{split}
        \min_d ~& \frac{1}{2}d^T\left (\nabla^2 f(x_k) + \sigma_k I \right ) d + \nabla f(x_k)^T d\\ \operatorname{s.t.} ~& \|d\| \le r_k.
    \end{split}
\end{equation}

As shown, this new formulation incorporates both an $\ell_2$-regularizer and the TR constraint. This modification makes the TR subproblem more flexible, allowing for the adjustment of the iteration-dependent parameters $\sigma_k$ and $r_k$. Notably, by setting $\sigma_k = 0$ and appropriately choosing $r_k$ based on the classic ratio test, we recover the standard TR subproblem. With careful selection of $\sigma_k$ and $r_k$, the modified subproblem becomes a powerful tool for enhancing the theoretical properties of the TR-type method.

The optimality conditions for this modified subproblem are similar to those in the classical TR method as described in~\citet[Section 3]{conn2000trust}, so we omit the proof for brevity.

\begin{lemma}\label{lem.optimal condition}
    The direction $d_k$ is the solution to \eqref{eq.newTR} if and only if there exists a dual multiplier $\lambda_k \geq 0 $ such that: \begin{subequations}
        \begin{align}
            \label{eq.optcond primal}
             & \| d_k\| \leq r_k                                                             \\
            \label{eq.optcond coml slack}
             & \lambda_k \left(\|d_k\|-r_k \right)=0                                         \\ \label{eq.optcond firstorder}
             & \left(\nabla^2 f(x_k) + \sigma_k I + \lambda_k I \right) d_k = -\nabla f(x_k) \\
            \label{eq.optcond secondorder}
             & \nabla^2 f(x_k) + \sigma_k I + \lambda_k I \succeq 0.
        \end{align}
    \end{subequations}
\end{lemma}

For convenience, we denote the exact primal-dual solution pair of \eqref{eq.newTR} as $(d_k,\lambda_k) = \operatorname{UTR}(x_k, \sigma_k, r_k)$.

Similar to the update rule in~\eqref{eq.optcond firstorder}, most second-order methods update the iterates in the following form:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq.second-order update}
    \left (\nabla^2 f(x_k) + \mu_k I \right )d_k = -\nabla f(x_k),
\end{equation}
where $\mu_k$ is a parameter that plays a crucial role in the complexity analysis and varies depending on the specific method. In the case of the new TR subproblem~\eqref{eq.newTR}, we have $\mu_k = \sigma_k + \lambda_k$, where $\lambda_k$ is the posterior multiplier. The introduction of the multiplier $\lambda_k$ adds a layer of complexity to the convergence analysis. We now briefly discuss the source of this difficulty.

A direct consequence of Assumption~\ref{assm.main assm} is that we can establish an upper bound for $f(x)$, as shown in~\citet[Lemma 4.1.1]{nesterov_lectures_2018}: \begin{equation*}
    f(x_k + d) \leq f(x_k) + \nabla f(x_k)^T d + \frac{1}{2}d^T \nabla^2 f(x_k) d + \frac{M}{6}\|d\|^3. \end{equation*}
Substituting \eqref{eq.second-order update} into this expression and noting that $\nabla^2 f(x_k) \succeq 0$, we obtain:
\begin{equation*}
    f(x_k + d_k) \leq f(x_k) - \mu_k \|d_k\|^2 + \frac{M}{6}\|d_k\|^3.
\end{equation*}
On one hand, monotonicity is essential for the global convergence of the method, which requires that $\mu_k \geq \frac{6}{M}\|d_k\|$. This further leads to the function value decrease being proportional to $\Theta(\|d_k\|^3)$.

On the other hand, from~\eqref{eq.second-order update}, we have:
\begin{equation*}
    \mu_k \|d_k\| \leq \|\left(\nabla^2 f(x_k) + \mu_k I\right) d_k\| = \|\nabla f(x_k)\|.
\end{equation*}
The above equation naturally describes the interplay between the multiplier, step size, and gradient norm. To achieve a more aggressive function value decrease, it is crucial to ensure that $\|d_k\|$ does not become too small. Therefore, the property $\mu_k = \Theta(\|d_k\|)$ is desirable for second-order methods.

For the cubic Newton method, such property always holds since the optimality condition of the subproblem guarantees that $\mu_k = \frac{M}{2}\|d_k\|$.However, there are no theoretical results that confirm the same property holds for TR-type methods. The results shown in Figure support our arguments.

Fortunately, although this favorable property is not inherently guaranteed for TR-type methods, there is a way to overcome this challenge. Specifically, the progress made in our new formulation~\eqref{eq.newTR} can be interpreted in two distinct ways. As demonstrated in the following two lemmas: when $\lambda_k>0$, we achieve a sufficient decrease in the function value; when $\lambda_k = 0$, we establish a bound on the gradient norm for the next iteration.

\begin{lemma}
    \label{lem.function decrease}
    Suppose Assumption~\ref{assm.main assm} holds and $(d_k,\lambda_k) = \operatorname{UTR}(x_k,\sigma_k,r_k)$. When $\sigma_k \geq M r_k$ and $\lambda_k>0$, we have
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq.function decrease advanced}
        f(x_k+d_k) \leq f(x_k)-\frac{1}{2}d_k^T \nabla^2 f(x_k) d_k-\lambda_k r_k^2 -\frac{5M}{6} r_k^3.
    \end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{lemma}
    \label{lem.bound next gradient}
    Suppose Assumption~\ref{assm.main assm} holds and $(d_k,\lambda_k)= \operatorname{UTR}(x_k,\sigma_k,r_k)$ , when $\lambda_k=0$, we have
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq.bound next gradient}
        \|\nabla f(x_k+d_k)\| \leq \frac{M}{2}r_k^2+\sigma_kr_k.
    \end{equation}
\end{lemma}

Since TR-type methods make per-iteration progress in a twofold manner, the estimating sequence technique~\cite{nesterov_lectures_2018}, which emphasizes function value reduction, cannot be directly applied. We briefly review this technique in a simplified manner here.

The main idea behind the estimating sequence technique is to maintain a dynamic sequence $\{\phi_k(x),A_k,v_k,x_k\}$ to approximate the objective function $f(x)$ from both above and below. In each iteration, we only update $x_k$ and $v_k$, while the auxiliary sequences $\phi_k(x)$ and $A_k$ are used for theoretical analysis and do not explicitly appear in the algorithm. Specifically, the following relations must be satisfied:
\begin{align}
    \label{eq.es relation 1}
     & \mathcal{R}_k^1: \ \phi_k^*  \geq A_k f(x_k), \  \phi_k^*= \phi_k(v_k) =\min_{x\in\mathbb{R}^n}\phi_k(x) \\
    \label{eq.es relation 2}
     & \mathcal{R}_k^2: \ \phi_k(x)\leq A_kf(x)+\phi_0(x), \ \forall x\in\mathbb{R}^n.
\end{align}
By setting $x = x^*$ in \eqref{eq.es relation 2}, it follows from \eqref{eq.es relation 1} and \eqref{eq.es relation 2} that
\begin{equation*}
    f(x_k)-f^* \leq \frac{1}{A_k}\phi_0(x^*).
\end{equation*}
Thus, the algorithm’s complexity is determined by how rapidly $A_k$ grows. For the accelerated cubic Newton method, \citet{nesterov2008accelerating} proposed an efficient way to update the aforementioned sequence and demonstrated that $A_k=\Theta(k^3)$ , leading to a complexity of $O(\epsilon^{-1/3})$.

As can be observed, the estimating sequence technique captures a recursive relationship between the function value across iterations. However, as discussed above, the TR-type method does not guarantee a consistent decrease in function value at every iteration. This limitation calls for a new design tailored to accelerate TR-type methods.

\section{The Accelerated Trust-Region(ATR) Method}
In this section, we propose the ATR method(Algorithm~\ref{alg.accelerated utr}) along with its auxiliary subroutine(Algorithm~\ref{alg.bisection}) and explain our intuition in designing them.

\begin{algorithm}[ht]
    \caption{Accelerated Trust-Region Method \atr{}}\label{alg.accelerated utr}
    \begin{algorithmic}[1]
        \STATE \textbf{input:} $x_0=v_0\in \mathbb{R}^n$, $s_0=0$
        \FOR{$k=0, 1, \ldots, \infty$}
        \STATE $y_k = \frac{k}{k+3}x_k+\frac{3}{k+3}v_k$
        \STATE $(d_k,\lambda_k) = \operatorname{UTR}(y_k,\frac{\sqrt{M}}{3}\|\nabla f(y_k)\|^{1/2},\frac{1}{3\sqrt{M}}\|\nabla f(y_k)\|^{1/2})$
        \IF{$\lambda_k>0$} \label{line.if}
        \STATE $x_{k+1}=y_k+d_k$ \label{line.nonzero multiplier}
        \STATE $s_k = s_{k-1}+\frac{(k+1)(k+2)}{2}\nabla f(y_k)$
        \ELSE \label{line.else}
        \STATE $d_k^{(0)}=d_k$, $\lambda_k^{(0)} = \lambda_k$, $j=0$
        \WHILE{$\lambda_k^{(j)}=0$} \label{line.while}
        \STATE $j=j+1$
        \STATE $(d_k^{(j)},\lambda_k^{(j)})=\operatorname{UTR}(y_k,\frac{\sqrt{M}}{3}\|\nabla f(y_k+d_k^{(j-1)})\|^{1/2},\frac{1}{3\sqrt{M}}\|\nabla f(y_k+d_k^{(j-1)})\|^{1/2})$
        \ENDWHILE \label{line.endwhile}
        \IF{$\lambda_k^{(j)}\leq \frac{\sqrt{M}}{3}\|\nabla f(y_k+d_k^{(j-1)})\|^{1/2}$} \label{line.bisection}
        \STATE $x_{k+1} = y_k+d_k^{(j)}$ \label{line.zero multiplier 1}
        \ELSE
        \STATE $\mu_- = \frac{\sqrt{M}}{3}\|\nabla f(y_k+d_k^{(j-1)})\|^{1/2}$, $\mu_+ = \lambda_k^{(j)}+\frac{\sqrt{M}}{3}\|\nabla f(y_k+d_k^{(j-1)})\|^{1/2}$ \label{line.bracketing}
        \STATE $x_{k+1} = \operatorname{BISECTION\_ATR}(y_k,\mu_-,\mu_+)$ \label{line.zero multiplier 2}
        \ENDIF \label{line.endbisection}
        \STATE $s_k=s_{k-1}+\frac{(k+1)(k+2)}{2}\nabla f(x_{k+1})$
        \ENDIF
        \STATE $v_{k+1} = v_0-\sqrt{\frac{\ell}{3M\|s_k\|}}s_k$
        \ENDFOR
    \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[!ht]
    \caption{Bisection for ATR}\label{alg.bisection}
    \begin{algorithmic}[1]
        \STATE{\textbf{input:} $y\in \mathbb{R}^n$, $\mu_- < \mu_+$;}
        \WHILE{True}
        \STATE{$\mu = \frac{\mu_-+\mu_+}{2}$}
        \STATE{$d = -\left (\nabla ^2 f(y)+\mu I\right )^{-1}\nabla f(y)$}
        \IF{$\frac{\mu}{\|d\|}<M$}
        \STATE{$\mu_-=\mu$}
        \ELSIF{$\frac{\mu}{\|d\|}>2M$}
        \STATE{$\mu_+ = \mu$}
        \ELSE
        \STATE{\textbf{output} $y+d,\mu$}
        \ENDIF
        \ENDWHILE
    \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

Following the instructions of Lemma~\ref{lem.function decrease} and Lemma~\ref{lem.bound next gradient}, a natural idea is to classify the iterations into two categories and deal with them separately: one with nonzero multiplier which decrease the function value sufficiently and another with zero multiplier which makes the gradient norm contract.

Specifically, in Algorithm~\ref{alg.accelerated utr} there are two criteria to perform updates on $x_k$ based on the information of the multiplier $\lambda_k$ at line~\ref{line.if} and line~\ref{line.else}: at line~\ref{line.if}, the function value decrease is sufficient since $\lambda_k>0$, the recursive relation in function value can be easily passed to the next iteration, therefore we can directly perform the update.

However, at line~\ref{line.else}, the case $\lambda_k = 0$ calls for different processing. An advantage in this case is that the gradient norm of the next iterate will show a linear contraction.

\begin{lemma}
    \label{lem.inner loop bound next gradient}
    Suppose Assumption~\ref{assm.main assm} holds, in $k$-th outer loop and $j$-th inner loop, if $\lambda_k^{(j)}=0$, then we have
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq.inner loop bound next gradient}
        \|\nabla f(y_k+d_k^{(j)}) \| \leq \frac{1}{18}\|\nabla f(y_k+d_k^{(j-1)})\|.
    \end{equation}
\end{lemma}

Intuitively, we don't want to let this kind of contraction slip away. Therefore we design the 'while' loop at line~\ref{line.while} to line~\ref{line.endwhile}. There are two possible outcomes of this loop, the first is that the multiplier still equals $0$, as a result, the gradient norm keeps contracting until an $\epsilon$-approximate solution is found.

The second is that the multiplier no longer equals zero, as a result, we have
\begin{equation*}
    \begin{aligned}
         & \|d_k^{(j)}\| = \frac{1}{3\sqrt{M}}\|f(y_k+d_k^{(j-1)})\|^{1/2},                                  \\
         & \mu_k^{(j)}:= \sigma_k^{(j)}+\lambda_k^{(j)} \geq \frac{\sqrt{M}}{3}\|f(y_k+d_k^{(j-1)})\|^{1/2}.
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation*}
Hence we are provided with a pair of 'good predict' of $\sigma_k^{(j)}$ and $r_k^{(j)}$ such that the aforementioned relation $\mu_k^{(j)} = \Theta(\|d_k^{(j)}\|)$ roughly holds. Then a transparent search procedure at line~\ref{line.bisection} to line~\ref{line.endbisection} is conducted to correct the parameters to make the relation hold strictly, where Algorithm~\ref{alg.bisection} serves as a basic bisection tool.

At the end of each iteration, we update the sequence $v_k$ based on the previous information of $s_k$, which guarantees that the recursive relation \eqref{eq.es relation 1} and \eqref{eq.es relation 2} hold.
\section{Convergence Analysis}
We denote the first index satisfies Definition~\ref{def.approximate solution} as $K_\epsilon$, in this section we will show our main theorem, which demonstrates that Algorithm~\ref{alg.accelerated utr} finds an $\epsilon$-approximate solution in $O(\epsilon^{-1/3})$ outer iterations and the total TR subproblem solved will not exceed $\tilde O(\epsilon^{-1/3})$.

First, we clarify that there are two ways of finding an $\epsilon$-approximate solution in our algorithm as discussed in the previous section. The first way is using the linear contraction property exhibited in the 'while' loop. It is good to see this case happen, whenever this case happens before iteration $K \leq O(\epsilon^{-1/3})$, the algorithm is early terminated.

When the algorithm does not terminate in the first way in $O(\epsilon^{-1/3})$ iterations, the algorithm is otherwise guaranteed to converge to $\epsilon$-approximate solutions by the estimating sequence technique: the main iterate sequence $x_k$ is capable of inheriting the crucial recursive relation, and thus the complexity results are derived by the growth rate of $A_k$.

If the algorithm does not terminate in the first way, we shall show that the 'while' loop at line~\ref{line.while} will terminate in $O(\log 1/\epsilon)$ iterations.

\begin{lemma}
    \label{lem.inner loop terminate}
    In the $k$-th outer loop of Algorithm~\ref{alg.accelerated utr}, the inner loop will terminate in
    \begin{equation*}
        j_k \leq O\left (\log \frac{\|\nabla f(y_k)\|}{\epsilon}\right)
    \end{equation*}
    iterations, otherwise we find a point such that
    \begin{equation*}
        \| \nabla f(y_k+d_k^{(j_k)})\| \leq \epsilon.
    \end{equation*}
    % Since the inner loop will terminate eventually, we can actually have a tighter bound for $j_k$:
    % \begin{equation}
    %     \label{eq.tighter bound jk}
    %     j_k \leq O\left (\log \frac{\|\nabla f(y_k)\|}{\|\nabla f(y_k+d_k^{(j_k-1)})\|}\right)
    % \end{equation}
\end{lemma}

Then we consider the complexity of the bisection procedure, we remark that the goal of the bisection search is to find a pair of $\mu_k,d_k$ with $\mu_k = \Theta(\|d_k\|)$. In particular, our choice of parameters in Algorithm~\ref{alg.accelerated utr} and Algorithm~\ref{alg.bisection} target at finding
\begin{equation}\label{eq.goal of bisection 1}
    M\|d_k\| \leq \mu_k\leq 2M\|d_k\|,
\end{equation}
we define the auxiliary function for iteration $k$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq.auxiliary bisection function}
    g_{y_k}(\mu): = \frac{\mu}{\|\left(\nabla^2 f(y_k)+\mu I\right)^{-1}\nabla f(y_k)\|}.
\end{equation}
Therefore, the goal \eqref{eq.goal of bisection 1} is equivalent to
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq.goal of bisection 2}
    M\leq g_{y_k}(\mu)\leq 2M.
\end{equation}
The following lemma claims that we have found the bracketing points for the bisection procedure at line~\ref{line.bracketing}.

\begin{lemma}
    \label{eq.valid bracketing}
    In the $k$-th iteration, at line~\ref{line.bracketing} of Algorithm~\ref{alg.accelerated utr},
    \begin{equation*}
        g_{y_k}(\mu_-) < M, \ g_{y_k}(\mu_+) >2M.
    \end{equation*}
\end{lemma}

Also, to guarantee the validity of the bisection search, we establish the following propositions of $g_{y_k}(\mu)$ over the interval $\left[\mu_-,\mu_+\right]$.
\begin{lemma}
    \label{lem.property g}
    For any $k\geq0$, $g_{y_k}(\mu)$ is continuously differentiable and monotonically increasing for $\mu \in \left (0,+\infty\right ]$ and its derivative is uniformly bounded above.
\end{lemma}
The above lemmas are crucial in analyzing the complexity of the bisection procedure, with the help of them we have
\begin{lemma}
    \label{lem.bound initial interval}
    At the $k$-th iteration of Algorithm~\ref{alg.accelerated utr}, the complexity of the bisection search procedure is at most $O(\log \frac{\|\nabla f(y_k)\|}{\epsilon})$.
\end{lemma}


Next, we will show how we can apply the estimating sequence technique to analyze the complexity of our algorithm. To begin with, we present how we set the auxiliary sequence $A_k$ and $\phi_k(x)$. Our approach is different from the standard one introduced in~\citet{nesterov_lectures_2018} in that we make it more suitable for our method.

We recursively define
\begin{align}
    \label{eq.update a and A}
     & a_k = \frac{(k+1)(k+2)}{2}, \ A_k=\frac{k(k+1)(k+2)}{6} \\
    \label{eq.update phi}
     & \phi_0(x) = \frac{M}{\ell}\|x-x_0\|^3,                  \\
     & \phi_{k+1}(x) =\left\{
    \begin{aligned}
         & \phi_k(x)+a_k\left (f(x_k)+\langle \nabla f(x_k),x-x_k\rangle \right ), k\in \mathcal{N} \\
         & \phi_k(x)+a_k\left (f(y_k)+\langle \nabla f(y_k),x-y_k\rangle \right ), k\in \mathcal{Z}
    \end{aligned}
    \right.
\end{align}
where the sets
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq.outer index catagory}
    \begin{aligned}
         & \mathcal{Z} = \left \{ k | x_k \text{\ updated at line~\ref{line.nonzero multiplier}}\right\},                                    \\
         & \mathcal{N} = \left \{k | x_k \text{\ updated at line~\ref{line.zero multiplier 1} or line~\ref{line.zero multiplier 2}}\right\}.
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}
The sequences defined above and the iterates produced by Algorithm~\ref{alg.accelerated utr} satisfy the following
\begin{lemma}
    \label{lem.property of phi}
    For the function sequence $\phi_k(x)$, they have the following properties
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item $v_k$ is the unique minimizer of $\phi_k(x)$, i.e.,
              \begin{equation*}
                  \phi_k^* =\min \phi_k(x) = \phi_k(v_k).
              \end{equation*}
        \item Suppose Assumption~\ref{assm.main assm} holds, set $0<\ell\leq \frac{25}{34992}$, if Algorithm~\ref{alg.accelerated utr} does not find $\epsilon$-approximate solutions in the 'while' loop, then $\mathcal{R}_k^1$ and $\mathcal{R}_k^2$ defined in \eqref{eq.es relation 1} and \eqref{eq.es relation 2} hold for all $k\geq0$.
    \end{enumerate}
\end{lemma}

As a direct consequence of the above lemma, the complexity of Algorithm~\ref{alg.accelerated utr} follows,

\begin{theorem}
    \label{thm.convergence outer loop}
    Suppose \autoref{assm.main assm} holds, then \autoref{alg.accelerated utr} find a point $x\in\mathbb{R}^n$ satisfies Definition~\ref{def.approximate solution} in $O(\epsilon^{-1/3})$ outer iterations. Moreover, the number of the TR subproblems solved is bounded by $\tilde O(\epsilon^{-1/3})$.
\end{theorem}
\section{Numerical Experiments}
In this section, we implement the universal trust-region method (\utr{}) and its accelerated enhancement (\atr{}) to compare with the cubic regularization method (\arc{}) and its accelerated version (\arca{}). Due to space constraints, only a brief description of the experiment is provided here; further details and the exact experimental setup can be found in \Cref{sec.experiments}.

We begin by testing performance on Nesterov's lower bound problem \cite{nesterovImplementableTensorMethods2021}:
\[
    f_k(x) = \eta_{p+1}\left(A_k x\right) - \left\langle e_1, x\right\rangle, \quad 2 \leq k \leq p,
\]
where $\eta_{p+1}(x) = \tfrac{1}{p+1}\|x\|_{p+1}^{p+1}$, and $A_k$ is a parametrized bidiagonal matrix (details in \Cref{sec.experiments.nesterov}). This function becomes increasingly challenging as \(k\) increases.

For this experiment, we fix the problem size and differentiability order, considering \(k = 50, 100\). As shown in \Cref{fig.nesterov}, accelerated methods outperform their original variants, with \atr{} slightly outperforming \arca{} in both cases.

Next, we consider softmax minimization, which approximates piecewise linear functions:
\[
    f(x) := \mu \ln \left(\sum_{i=1}^m \exp \left(\tfrac{a_i^Tx - b_i}{\mu}\right)\right) \approx \max_{1 \leq i \leq m} \left\{a_i^T x - b_i\right\}.
\]
When \(\mu\) is small, this problem is notoriously challenging for unaccelerated methods due to the large smoothness constant, where \(M \leq \tfrac{12}{\mu^2}\) \cite{doikov_super-universal_2024}.

\section{Conclusion}
In this work, we addressed the challenge of accelerating trust-region-type methods, bridging the gap between their practical success and theoretical development. By proposing a novel framework that effectively balances function value reduction and gradient norm contraction, we extended the scope of existing acceleration techniques to accommodate the constrained nature of trust-region subproblems. Numerical experiments further support our theoretical findings.
